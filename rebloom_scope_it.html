re:Bloom Infant and Toddler Scope and Sequence Birth through 36 Months
Introduction

Curriculum Philosophy and Research Base

re:Bloom's Infant and Toddler Scope and Sequence covers children from birth through 36 months across all nine developmental domains. Infant and toddler development are presented together in a single document because, as Creative Curriculum and the research base both affirm, development across this age span is continuous rather than categorical. The indicators within each standard are differentiated by age band: Birth to 12 Months and 12 to 36 Months. Educators should use professional observation and family knowledge to identify where each child is currently functioning and use the appropriate band as the reference for planning and assessment.

The theoretical foundation of this document draws on the most research-supported frameworks in infant-toddler development. John Bowlby's attachment theory and Mary Ainsworth's empirical research on the strange situation establish secure attachment as the primary developmental organizing system of the first three years. Lev Vygotsky's sociocultural theory shapes our understanding that cognition develops first in relationship before it becomes internal; serve-and-return interaction is not supplemental to learning but its primary mechanism. Jean Piaget's sensorimotor stage describes the first two years as a period of learning through the body: touching, mouthing, dropping, and manipulating are the curriculum. Maria Montessori's principles of the prepared environment and intrinsic motivation inform our approach to setting up beautiful, inviting, accessible spaces for infants and toddlers to explore independently. The Harvard Center on the Developing Child provides the neurobiological grounding for our emphasis on relationships, executive function, and stress regulation as prerequisites for all other development.

Science of Reading principles are embedded throughout all nine domains, not only Language and Literacy. The SoR foundation for infants and toddlers is oral language: the richness of vocabulary exposure, the quality of conversational interaction, and the density of phonologically interesting language (songs, rhymes, and books) in the first three years predict reading achievement more reliably than any kindergarten instructional intervention. re:Bloom treats every diapering conversation, every lullaby, and every board book read-aloud as literacy instruction because the research confirms that they are.

Trauma-informed care principles are woven into every domain of this document. The first three years are the period of maximum neurological sensitivity to the effects of adversity, neglect, and chronic stress. Children who have experienced early trauma may present with behaviors that are frequently misread: avoidance of exploration, difficulty with transitions, dysregulation, aggression, or flat affect. These are neurological adaptations, not character problems. The response to every such presentation is increased warmth, predictability, and relational investment.

Daily Caregiving and Learning Schedule

The following schedule framework reflects the re:Bloom approach to structuring the infant-toddler day. Infant schedules must be individually responsive to each child's feeding, sleeping, and activity rhythms and cannot be imposed as a group schedule. Toddler schedules can be more consistent while still accommodating individual variation.

Assessment Overview

Assessment of infants and toddlers is always observational, relationship-based, and ongoing. Standardized assessments are not appropriate for children under three years of age except for developmental screening purposes conducted by trained specialists. The re:Bloom Infant and Toddler Assessment System includes the following components:

Daily Anecdotal Records

Brief written observations recorded during caregiving and play. Focus on what the child does, says, and communicates rather than what they cannot do. Anecdotes are organized by domain code and filed in each child's portfolio folder.

Child Observation and Assessment Tool

The re:Bloom Child Observation and Assessment Tool is completed three times per year using the Birth to 12 Months or 12 to 36 Months indicators for each standard. Results generate an AI-written developmental summary and next steps. Full administration instructions are in the re:Bloom Assessment Guide.

Portfolio Documentation

Each child's portfolio includes dated photographs with caregiver notations, work samples where applicable, and the completed assessment at each rating period. Portfolios are shared with families at scheduled conferences and used as the basis for individualized planning.

Family Communication

Daily verbal and written communication with families about what their infant or toddler explored, said, or accomplished is a component of the re:Bloom assessment system. Families are the primary experts on their child's development; their knowledge informs both assessment and planning.

AL

Approaches to Learning

Infant and Toddler Track · Birth through 36 Months · 4 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Approaches to Learning in the first three years of life is inseparable from the quality of relationships. Infants and toddlers do not approach new experiences in a vacuum; they approach them through the lens of felt safety, caregiver responsiveness, and secure attachment. John Bowlby's attachment theory and Mary Ainsworth's research on the secure base establish that exploration, curiosity, and persistence in young children depend on the degree to which caregivers have established themselves as reliable, warm, and consistent sources of comfort. A toddler who trusts their caregiver will move away from them to investigate a new toy. One who does not will cling, avoid, or collapse. This is not temperament; it is neurobiology. Howard Gardner's concept of multiple intelligences is visible even in infancy: some babies are drawn to faces (interpersonal), others to sounds (musical), others to moving objects (spatial). Approaching the world is the beginning of intelligence, and each child's mode of approach is worth observing, honoring, and building upon. Trauma-informed care is critical throughout this domain: children who have experienced early adversity, neglect, or inconsistent caregiving may exhibit what looks like low curiosity or avoidance of novelty. These responses are adaptive, not deficits. The path to rebuilding approach and exploration runs through relationship, not through stimulation.

SoR
SoR connection: Curiosity, attention, and persistence are the foundational learning behaviors that support all early literacy development. A toddler who maintains focused engagement with a board book, who reaches for it again and again, is developing the sustained attention and intrinsic motivation that learning to read will require.
TIC
Trauma-informed note: Avoidance of new experiences, low persistence, and flat affect in infants and toddlers are among the earliest indicators of stress dysregulation. Meet these children with calm, warmth, and predictability rather than stimulation or redirection. Curiosity follows safety.
AL.1.a Engages in new and unfamiliar experiences. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Shows interest in new objects placed nearby or within visual field
  • Reaches toward and mouths new materials as primary exploration
  • Repeats actions that produce interesting results (batting a mobile, splashing water)
  • Shows brief wariness of unfamiliar people before returning to exploration with caregiver present
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Approaches new materials and activities with visible excitement or curiosity
  • Imitates new skills or actions demonstrated by a familiar adult
  • Repeats a new activity many times to develop confidence and mastery
  • Seeks caregiver reassurance when uncertain, then re-engages with the new experience
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Introduce one new object or experience at a time; allow full exploration before rotating
  • 2Use Montessori-inspired 'invitations': arrange intriguing materials near children without directing how to use them
  • 3Stay nearby during exploration; your calm presence is the permission to investigate
  • 4Narrate what you observe: 'You found the bumpy side. Now you are turning it over.'
  • 5For hesitant children, explore the new material yourself first so they can watch before touching
AL.1.b Attempts and repeats activities with increasing complexity. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Repeats a purposeful action many times (dropping a block, banging a surface)
  • Follows through on a simple one-step sequence with adult support
  • Shows frustration or persistence when an object is just out of reach
  • Moves through familiar routines with increasing anticipation of next steps
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Attempts two-step familiar routines without adult initiation
  • Returns to an unfinished activity after interruption
  • Tries multiple approaches when first attempt does not work
  • Sequences familiar activities in correct order (gets bib, then sits at table)
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Avoid completing tasks for children; presence and encouragement are enough
  • 2Provide simple cause-and-effect toys that reward persistence: pop-up toys, stacking rings, nesting cups
  • 3Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development: stay just above current ability, not far above it
  • 4When a toddler gives up, say 'You almost got it. Want to try once more?' rather than taking over
AL.1.c Sustains attention and persists in completing activities. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Maintains focused attention on a preferred object or face for a sustained period
  • Returns gaze or reach to an object after a brief distraction
  • Shows satisfaction (kicking, vocalizing) when an action produces an expected result
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Sustains engagement with a self-chosen activity for five or more minutes
  • Returns to a challenging task after a brief break
  • Expresses visible pride or satisfaction when completing something difficult
  • Resists redirection during deep engagement with a preferred activity
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Honor uninterrupted play: do not redirect a child who is deeply engaged for the sake of routine unless necessary
  • 2Observe what captures sustained attention for each child; that is curriculum information
  • 3Use specific encouragement: 'You have been working on that tower for a long time' rather than 'Good job'
  • 4Dweck's research on growth mindset applies even to toddlers: name effort, not outcome
AL.2.a Develops a beginning orientation toward learning and growth. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Responds positively to adult encouragement during exploration
  • Attempts actions that are slightly beyond current ability (reaching a bit farther, pulling to stand)
  • Shows pride through vocalization or expression after achieving something new
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Says or implies 'I can do it' when attempting a challenge
  • Tries again after a setback with caregiver encouragement
  • Notices and names growing abilities: 'I jumped!' 'I did it myself!'
  • Begins to distinguish between things they can do now and things they are still learning
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Name growth explicitly and often: 'Last week you could not snap that; now you can'
  • 2Avoid excessive praise; specific noticing is more powerful than 'You are so smart'
  • 3Model a learning orientation yourself: 'I am going to try something tricky. I might need to try more than once'
  • 4Celebrate effort and process; finished products matter far less than the attempt
CO

Cognitive Development

Infant and Toddler Track · Birth through 36 Months · 4 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Cognitive development in the first three years is rooted in the body, the senses, and the relationship. Jean Piaget described the first two years as the sensorimotor stage: infants and young toddlers build their entire understanding of the world through direct sensory and motor experience. Object permanence, the understanding that objects continue to exist when out of sight, is one of the most significant cognitive achievements of the first year. By 18 to 24 months, children enter the preoperational stage and begin using symbols: words stand for objects, gestures stand for actions, one object stands for another in play. This symbolic capacity is the cognitive foundation of language, literacy, and mathematics. Lev Vygotsky's contribution to understanding infant-toddler cognition is the fundamental role of social interaction: infants learn to think through and with their caregivers. Joint attention, the shared focus of adult and child on the same object or event, is among the most powerful predictors of language and cognitive development in the first two years. The Harvard Center on the Developing Child describes serve-and-return interactions as building the neural architecture of learning itself; each contingent caregiver response is a brick in the structure of cognition. Executive function, including working memory, attention, and impulse control, begins its development in infancy and is shaped profoundly by the predictability and responsiveness of the caregiving environment.

SoR
SoR connection: Symbolic thinking, the understanding that one thing can represent another, is the direct cognitive precursor to the alphabetic principle. A toddler who uses a block as a phone is doing the same cognitive work as a reader who understands that the letter B represents the sound /b/.
CO.1.a Develops the ability to recall and recognize people, objects, and experiences. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Recognizes primary caregiver by sight, sound, and smell
  • Shows recognition of frequently used objects (bottle, pacifier, favorite toy)
  • Anticipates familiar routines and events (excitement at seeing the bib)
  • Shows object permanence: looks for a toy briefly hidden under a cloth
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Remembers where familiar objects are kept and retrieves them
  • Recalls events from earlier in the day when prompted by a familiar adult
  • Identifies familiar people and objects in photographs
  • Re-enacts familiar routines in play (feeds doll, puts it to bed)
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Use consistent language during routines so children build predictable mental maps: 'Now we wash hands, then we eat'
  • 2Play simple hide-and-seek games with objects to build object permanence from four months onward
  • 3Photograph meaningful experiences and review them with children; this builds narrative memory
  • 4Joint attention during caregiving is the most natural memory-building activity: name and point to everything
CO.2.a Develops the capacity for symbolic and representational thinking. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Uses a familiar object functionally (holds cup to mouth, spoon to food)
  • Begins to use gestures to represent requests (reaching up means 'pick me up')
  • Responds to pictures in board books as representations of real things
  • Imitates familiar actions after a brief delay (deferred imitation)
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Uses one object to represent another in play (a block becomes a phone)
  • Engages in brief two- to three-step pretend play sequences
  • Names objects in photographs and simple illustrations
  • Begins to understand that print and numbers carry meaning
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Offer open-ended materials that invite substitution: scarves, blocks, containers of varied sizes
  • 2Model symbolic play: pick up a banana and say 'Hello? Hello?' to invite the child into the symbol
  • 3Respond to children's gestures as if they are words; this validates symbolic communication
  • 4Read board books daily; pointing to pictures while naming them builds representational knowledge
CO.3.a Uses increasingly purposeful strategies to achieve goals. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Tries a different approach when first attempt fails (reaches around an obstacle)
  • Uses a caregiver as a tool to achieve a goal (hands them the jar to open)
  • Repeatedly experiments with cause-and-effect relationships (drops food from highchair)
  • Persists in physical problem-solving with brief concentration
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Tries two or more approaches before seeking adult help
  • Asks for help using words or gestures when genuinely stuck
  • Applies a solution learned in one situation to a similar new situation
  • Begins to verbalize the problem before attempting to solve it
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Provide materials with multiple outcomes: nesting cups, shape sorters, simple puzzles with varied entry points
  • 2Wait before helping; count silently to ten before intervening
  • 3When a child asks for help, offer the minimum support needed: 'Try turning it the other way'
  • 4Name problem-solving explicitly: 'You figured that out. You tried three different ways'
CO.4.a Develops beginning cognitive flexibility and tolerance for change. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Adjusts behavior in response to consistent caregiver cues
  • Shifts visual attention from one object to another
  • Tolerates minor changes in routine with caregiver support and preparation
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Accepts changes in routine with advance notice and clear explanation
  • Shifts between two play activities with decreasing difficulty over time
  • Follows simple rules in one context even when they differ from another context
  • Tolerates a substitute caregiver with a familiar caregiver's reassurance and preparation
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Use visual and verbal transition warnings: 'Two more minutes at the water table, then we wash hands'
  • 2Prepare children for any change in routine before it happens, not during
  • 3Harvard's executive function research identifies flexibility as a trainable cognitive skill from infancy onward
  • 4Respond to emotional transitions with empathy first: 'You are not ready to stop. That was hard to leave'
CR

Creative Development

Infant and Toddler Track · Birth through 36 Months · 3 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Creative development in infancy and toddlerhood is primarily sensory, motor, and relational before it is artistic. A six-month-old who slaps their palm into a puddle of applesauce is engaged in creative exploration; so is the two-year-old who bangs a wooden spoon against every surface in the kitchen to compare sounds. Howard Gardner's theory of multiple intelligences is most directly visible in creative development: musical intelligence, bodily-kinesthetic intelligence, and spatial intelligence all emerge in observable form before the second birthday. Maria Montessori's prepared environment principle shapes this domain profoundly: when art materials, music, and movement opportunities are beautiful, accessible, and real, children engage with them with a depth and seriousness that plastic toy versions rarely inspire. Creative development is not a nice-to-have in infant-toddler programs; it is a primary pathway for sensory integration, emotional expression, language development, and early mathematical and scientific thinking. Process is everything at this age. The product is irrelevant. An infant who sweeps their hand through finger paint for the first time is not making art; they are discovering that their body has agency in the world.

SoR
SoR connection: Music and rhyme in the infant-toddler years build phonological awareness from the ground up. Songs, chants, and rhythmic language develop sensitivity to the sound structures of words before children have any explicit awareness that this is happening. Every lullaby is literacy instruction.
CR.1.a Explores and expresses through sensory and visual art experiences. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Explores art materials through touch, taste, and visual attention
  • Makes marks with a held crayon or marker using palmar grasp
  • Reacts visibly to colors, patterns, and textures in the environment
  • Shows sustained interest in looking at simple illustrations and images
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Makes intentional marks and lines on paper with a writing tool
  • Explores multiple art materials: paint, playdough, collage, markers
  • Chooses colors or materials with apparent intentionality
  • Names or points to what they have created when asked
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Offer sensory art materials daily: finger paint, playdough, sand, shaving cream on a tray
  • 2Use real materials, not toy versions: real paintbrushes, real clay, real fabric scraps
  • 3Describe what you see without evaluating: 'You used a lot of red. Your hand went all the way across'
  • 4Display children's work at their eye level with their name; this communicates that their making matters
  • 5Never show children 'what it should look like'; there is no model at this age
CR.1.b Expresses self through music, movement, and vocal play. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Responds to music with whole-body movement: kicking, rocking, reaching
  • Vocalizes along with songs and rhymes
  • Attends to singing faces and musical instruments with focused interest
  • Produces varied vocalizations including babbles that rise and fall in pitch
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Moves body to a musical beat with increasing coordination
  • Sings along with familiar songs, filling in words and phrases
  • Experiments with rhythm instruments: shaking, banging, tapping
  • Creates simple original songs or chants spontaneously during play
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Sing throughout the day: diapering, feeding, transitions, play; your voice is curriculum
  • 2Use call-and-response songs and fingerplays; these build phonological awareness and turn-taking simultaneously
  • 3Provide rhythm instruments that work: shakers with sound, drums that resonate, bells that ring
  • 4Move with infants in arms: rock, sway, bounce; embodied musical experience is the foundation of musical intelligence
  • 5Play varied music styles in the background; children deserve more than nursery rhymes
CR.2.a Develops imaginative and inventive play. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Imitates simple familiar actions (stirs with a spoon, holds a phone to ear)
  • Responds to adult's playful pretend actions with interest and imitation
  • Uses an object in its functional context during play (drinks from empty cup)
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Engages in pretend play sequences of two or more steps
  • Takes on a simple role in dramatic play: baby, dog, mommy
  • Uses one object to represent another within a play sequence
  • Invites caregivers or peers into pretend play with gestures or words
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Set up simple dramatic play invitations: baby doll with blanket and bottle, kitchen with pots and food
  • 2Follow the child's play lead; your role is to extend, not direct
  • 3Vivian Paley's work on the importance of story and play: narrate and extend what you observe rather than redirecting
  • 4Offer open-ended props rather than realistic toy sets; a scarf can be a cape, a sling, a river, or a tablecloth

Language and Literacy

Infant and Toddler Track · Birth through 36 Months · 12 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Language and literacy development in the first three years is among the most consequential developmental processes in human life. Hart and Risley's landmark research established that children who hear rich, varied language in the first three years arrive at school with dramatically larger vocabularies and stronger reading foundations than those who do not. The critical window for oral language development is open and highly sensitive from birth; this is not a metaphor but a neurological fact confirmed by decades of research in cognitive neuroscience. The Science of Reading framework, grounded in decades of rigorous research, identifies phonological awareness as one of the strongest predictors of reading success. Phonological awareness begins in infancy through exposure to the rhythmic, rhyming, and melodic qualities of infant-directed speech. Every time a caregiver sings a lullaby, says a nursery rhyme, or bounces a baby to a chant, they are building the phonological sensitivity that will support decoding years later. Dana Suskind's work at the University of Chicago (Thirty Million Words) documents the specific qualities of caregiver language that matter most: quantity, variety, and the degree to which language is contingent and conversational rather than directed and instructional. The most powerful language environment for an infant or toddler is not a flashcard program; it is a warm, responsive caregiver who talks about everything, all day, in real interaction.

SoR
SoR connection: This entire domain is grounded in Science of Reading research. Oral language (LL.1.a through LL.1.e) builds the language comprehension strand of the Reading Rope. Phonological awareness (LL.2.a through LL.2.c) builds the word recognition strand. Print concepts (LL.3.a, LL.3.b) and early writing (LL.4.a, LL.4.b) build alphabetic and orthographic knowledge. All four strands are seeded in the first three years.
LL.1.a Demonstrates understanding of increasingly complex language. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Turns head and body toward familiar voice
  • Responds to own name by four to six months
  • Follows simple one-word directions: 'up,' 'no,' 'more'
  • Responds to changes in adult tone: excited, soothing, warning
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Follows two-step directions in familiar contexts
  • Points to named pictures in board books
  • Responds appropriately to simple 'wh-' questions about the immediate environment
  • Understands simple negatives, location words, and action words
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Use precise language: not 'Come here' but 'Come to the sink to wash your hands'
  • 2Slow down and give processing time; toddlers need two to three seconds to understand before responding
  • 3Check comprehension through action, not repetition; ask children to show you rather than repeat back
  • 4Speak to infants as if they understand everything, because far more is registered than can yet be expressed
LL.1.b Develops vocabulary and conceptual knowledge. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Responds to frequently heard words with orienting behavior
  • Looks at named objects or people when language-object pairing is consistent
  • Associates words with familiar routines and objects
  • Responds differently to words with emotional valence: name, 'no,' 'milk'
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Understands 200 or more words by 24 months
  • Responds accurately to action words, descriptive words, and basic categories
  • Understands spatial terms in context: in, on, under, next to
  • Learns new words rapidly through a single exposure in meaningful context (fast mapping)
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Name everything all day; narrate your own actions and the child's: 'You are pushing the truck. The truck is going fast'
  • 2Beck, McKeown, and Kucan's Tier 1 vocabulary is the primary focus here: everyday concrete words used in meaningful context
  • 3Read books repeatedly; repetition of vocabulary in shared book reading is among the strongest vocabulary-building activities for this age
  • 4Expand and elaborate child language: when a toddler says 'dog,' say 'Yes, a big brown dog. He is running fast across the grass'
LL.1.c Communicates using increasingly complex language. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Coos and vocalizes in response to caregiver voices from birth
  • Uses varied consonant-vowel babbles by six to eight months
  • Produces intentional communicative gestures: reaching, pointing, waving
  • Uses first words with clear communicative intent between 10 and 14 months
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Uses 50 or more words by 18 to 24 months
  • Combines two words into meaningful phrases: 'more milk,' 'daddy go,' 'big dog'
  • Uses three-word sentences by 24 to 30 months
  • Speech is understood by familiar adults most of the time; unfamiliar adults understand 50 to 75 percent
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Respond to all communicative attempts including gestures, vocalizations, and gaze as if they were words
  • 2Serve and return: when a child vocalizes, pause, respond, and wait for their next turn
  • 3Model slightly more complex language than the child currently uses: if they say two words, you use three
  • 4Refer children to a speech-language pathologist if no words are present by 15 months or no two-word combinations by 24 months
LL.1.d Participates in early conversational turn-taking. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Engages in vocal back-and-forth with a familiar caregiver from birth
  • Pauses after vocalizing as if expecting a response
  • Initiates 'conversation' through eye contact, smiling, and vocalizing
  • Maintains joint attention on a shared object or event with a caregiver
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Initiates conversation to make requests, share observations, or seek help
  • Takes two or more conversational turns on the same topic
  • Stays on topic for two to three exchanges before shifting
  • Uses conversation to communicate needs, desires, and growing understanding of the world
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1The serve-and-return interaction is the most important language activity for infants and toddlers; prioritize it over any structured activity
  • 2Harvard's Center on the Developing Child: three to four serve-and-return interactions per minute with an engaged caregiver is the optimal language environment
  • 3Put down the phone, get on the floor, and make eye contact; this is the curriculum
  • 4Ask one genuine open-ended question at a time and wait for a real response before speaking again
LL.1.e Develops early comprehension of books and read-aloud text. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Attends to board book sharing for a brief time when held in caregiver's lap
  • Reaches for and explores board books as physical objects
  • Responds with excitement to familiar books through vocalization and movement
  • Attends to the pictures while the caregiver reads or points
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Turns pages one at a time and holds books correctly
  • Points to and names familiar pictures in books
  • Anticipates repeated phrases or events in familiar stories
  • Requests favorite books and shows preference for specific titles
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Read aloud every single day, multiple times per day; there is no upper limit
  • 2Use Dialogic Reading techniques: point, ask, expand; at the toddler level, PEER (Prompt, Evaluate, Expand, Repeat)
  • 3Let infants explore board books as physical objects; mouthing and banging a book is the beginning of book engagement
  • 4Choose books with rich vocabulary, simple stories, and beautiful illustrations; what you read matters
LL.2.a Develops early awareness of syllables and rhythm in speech. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Responds to the rhythmic qualities of speech and song
  • Attends to variations in vocal rhythm and prosody
  • Vocalizes with varied syllable patterns: ba-ba-ba, ma-ma-ma
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months

Mathematics

Infant and Toddler Track · Birth through 36 Months · 9 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Mathematical development in the first three years is embodied, relational, and play-based. Infants enter the world with a surprisingly sophisticated number sense: research by Karen Wynn and colleagues demonstrates that babies as young as five months notice when a visual set changes in number. This preverbal number sense is the biological seed from which mathematical reasoning grows. Clements and Sarama's learning trajectories framework establishes that mathematical development in early childhood follows predictable but individually paced pathways, and that the richest mathematical learning in infancy and toddlerhood happens through routine interaction with real objects, space, and quantity in everyday life. Piaget's sensorimotor stage is most relevant here: mathematical concepts like more and less, full and empty, big and small, and same and different are built through repeated, varied, hands-on experience with real materials. The adult's role is not to teach these concepts through instruction but to embed mathematical language in every interaction: at the changing table, at the sink, at mealtimes, during play. The richest mathematics classroom for a toddler is a well-stocked play environment with a caregiver who narrates everything with mathematical precision.

SoR
SoR connection: Number word knowledge develops through oral language. Counting songs, number rhymes, and mathematical vocabulary instruction directly reinforce phonological sensitivity and the vocabulary knowledge that supports reading comprehension.

Toddler — 12 to 36 Months

  • Points to objects while saying number words (without reliable one-to-one correspondence yet)

  • Participates enthusiastically in counting songs and fingerplays

  • 1Count everything during caregiving: steps on the stairs, crackers on the plate, buttons on the shirt
  • 2Counting songs are mathematical instruction: 'Five Little Monkeys,' 'One Two Three Four Five, Once I Caught a Fish Alive'
  • 3Use consistent counting language: always count in the same direction, always say the last number with emphasis
  • 4Clements and Sarama: stable order (the counting sequence never changes) is the first counting principle; build it through repetition and song

Toddler — 12 to 36 Months

  • 1Distribute snacks one at a time with counting: 'One cracker. Two crackers. Three crackers. You have three'

  • 3One-to-one correspondence is built through physical placement, not just verbal counting; use this distinction

  • 1Use dot cards with one, two, and three dots during play and transitions

  • 3Clements (1999): perceptual subitizing of one to three is the earliest form; conceptual subitizing develops later

PW

Physical Development and Wellness

Infant and Toddler Track · Birth through 36 Months · 10 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Physical development in infancy and toddlerhood is not preparation for later learning; it is learning itself. The brain develops through movement. Proprioception, vestibular processing, and sensorimotor integration are not peripheral biological functions; they are the neurological substrate of attention, executive function, and cognitive development. A. Jean Ayres' sensory integration theory, now supported by extensive neuroscience research, establishes that the body's ability to process and integrate sensory information is foundational to all higher-order learning. Tummy time is not a positioning exercise; it is core strength, vestibular development, and visual spatial processing. Walking is not just locomotion; it is spatial reasoning, executive function, and independence. Gross motor development in the first three years follows a predictable sequence but varies considerably in timing; the range of typical development is wide and caregivers should resist comparison among children. Fine motor development, including the grasp progression from palmar to pincer to tripod, is directly connected to later writing and self-care competence. Trauma-informed physical care is particularly important in this domain: children who have experienced physical abuse, neglect, or medical trauma may have altered relationships with their own bodies, physical contact, or sensory stimulation. Respond to all sensory and physical hesitation with patience, warmth, and choice rather than pressure.

SoR
SoR connection: Fine motor control (PW.1.b) develops the hand strength, grip stability, and directional control that efficient handwriting requires. Handwriting fluency, in turn, supports the writing-reading connection that strengthens encoding and ultimately decoding.
TIC
Trauma-informed note: Physical development and wellness in the first three years is inseparable from the safety and quality of the caregiving relationship. Feeding, diapering, bathing, and physical handling are not just caregiving tasks; they are the primary context in which trust, body safety, and physical self-knowledge are built. All physical caregiving should be warm, narrated, and consensual to the degree the child's development permits.

SC

Science

Infant and Toddler Track · Birth through 36 Months · 2 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Science in the first three years is not a content area; it is a way of being in the world. Infants are the most driven scientists on earth: they form hypotheses (What happens if I drop this?), test them (drops the spoon), observe the results (it falls and makes a noise), and repeat the experiment (drops it again, and again, and again) until the outcome is fully understood. Jean Piaget described this as circular reactions, the defining cognitive activity of the sensorimotor stage. Every time a caregiver resists the impulse to pick up the spoon and instead says 'It fell! What happened?' they are supporting the development of a scientist. The Reggio Emilia tradition treats young children as natural researchers whose hundred languages include touching, tasting, smelling, listening, watching, and wondering. Providing a rich sensory environment and asking genuine questions (not questions with predetermined answers) is the science curriculum for this age. Nature, water, sand, light, shadow, animals, and plants are the primary scientific domains of infant-toddler inquiry. Getting outside every day is science education. The National Research Council's Framework for K-12 Science Education (2012) establishes science and engineering practices as the core of science education; in infancy and toddlerhood, observing, questioning, and investigating are the practices that matter most.

SoR
SoR connection: Science builds content knowledge and vocabulary that are central to reading comprehension. The toddler who has watched water freeze, touched a live caterpillar, and grown a bean from seed has world knowledge that will make science texts comprehensible in ways that pure vocabulary instruction cannot replicate.

Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Mouths, shakes, bangs, and drops objects to explore their properties
  • Repeats actions that produce interesting results with clear intention
  • Explores natural materials with interest: leaves, grass, water, sand
  • Reaches for and explores objects of varied textures, weights, and temperatures
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Uses multiple senses systematically to explore materials
  • Pours, fills, stirs, and mixes substances in repetitive investigation
  • Compares two objects using simple descriptive language
  • Asks 'What's that?' and 'Why?' about unfamiliar phenomena
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Set up science invitations weekly: water with droppers, ice in warm water, sand with scoops, magnifying glasses with interesting objects
  • 2Resist explaining; ask 'What do you notice?' and 'What do you think will happen?' instead
  • 3Take infants and toddlers outside every day with time and freedom to observe; this is not optional
  • 4Science notebooks for toddlers: keep a simple observation book where you record and draw what children investigate each week
SC.1.b Develops early reasoning about cause and effect. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Repeats actions that produce a desired effect with clear intention
  • Shows a surprised response when an expected effect does not occur
  • Demonstrates understanding that their own actions produce predictable results
  • Explores cause-and-effect toys with sustained engagement
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Identifies a simple cause-and-effect relationship they have observed: 'It broke because I dropped it'
  • Predicts what will happen before a familiar action
  • Asks 'Why?' about observed phenomena with genuine curiosity
  • Connects an observed effect to its cause with adult prompting and scaffolding
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Cause-and-effect language is science language; use 'because,' 'causes,' 'makes,' and 'so that' during everyday caregiving
  • 2Ask predictive questions before familiar activities: 'What do you think will happen when we put the ice in the warm water?'
  • 3Honor the question 'Why?' with honest, simplified answers rather than deflection; curiosity is more important than the correct answer
  • 4NRC (2012): cause and effect is one of seven crosscutting concepts in science; building it in toddlerhood plants a lifelong scientific habit of mind
SE

Social-Emotional Development

Infant and Toddler Track · Birth through 36 Months · 7 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Social-emotional development in infancy and toddlerhood is not one developmental domain among many; it is the organizing system through which all other development occurs. The Harvard Center on the Developing Child's foundational research establishes that the serve-and-return interaction between infants and responsive caregivers is the single most powerful influence on brain architecture, stress response systems, and learning capacity. Secure attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth) identifies the quality of the caregiver-infant relationship as the primary predictor of social-emotional competence throughout life. Children who develop secure attachments in the first three years are more curious, more persistent, more socially competent, and more academically successful in school. This is not correlation; decades of longitudinal research confirm the mechanism. Trauma-informed practice is most essential in this domain: children who have experienced abuse, neglect, caregiver loss, or chronic family stress may arrive in care programs with insecure or disorganized attachment patterns that show up as aggression, withdrawal, excessive clinginess, or emotional dysregulation. These are not behavioral problems; they are communications about unmet developmental needs. The path forward is relationship, predictability, co-regulation, and time. CASEL's framework (self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, and responsible decision-making) provides useful language for the social-emotional competencies that emerge from secure, responsive caregiving in the first three years.

SoR
SoR connection: Self-regulation, the capacity to manage attention, impulse, and emotional state, is deeply connected to early literacy learning. Children who can sustain attention during a read-aloud, manage frustration during a difficult phonological awareness task, and re-engage after a distraction learn to read more readily than those who cannot (Blair and Razza, 2007).
TIC
Trauma-informed note: In this domain especially, behavior is communication. An infant who arches away from a caregiver, a toddler who bites when overwhelmed, a child who dissociates during group time; each is communicating something about their nervous system's history. The response is never punishment, redirection, or consequence. The response is calm, warm presence, co-regulation, and a sustained investment in relationship.
SE.1.a Develops a beginning sense of self as a unique and valued person. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Shows awareness of own body through mouthing, touching, and visual exploration
  • Smiles at own reflection in a mirror
  • Distinguishes familiar from unfamiliar people with different responses
  • Shows awareness of own emotional and physical states
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Refers to self by name and begins using 'I' and 'me'
  • Names and communicates basic personal characteristics: name, age, family members
  • Identifies own physical characteristics with some accuracy
  • Expresses preferences, opinions, and growing sense of personal identity
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Use the child's name constantly during caregiving; name-recognition is identity recognition
  • 2Post photographs of each child and their family at their eye level in the room
  • 3Anti-Bias Education (Derman-Sparks and Edwards, 2010): positive self-identity is built through consistent affirmation, representation, and belonging
  • 4Avoid comparative language: never 'She is better at that than you'; every child's self-perception is shaped by caregiver language
SE.1.b Develops early understanding of emotions in self and others. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Expresses emotions through facial expression, body, and vocalization
  • Responds differentially to caregiver emotional expressions
  • Shows clear differentiation between happy, distressed, and fearful states
  • Distinguishes between a smiling and a frowning face by two to three months
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Names happy, sad, mad, and scared in self and others
  • Identifies emotions in pictures, stories, and the faces of familiar people
  • Shows empathy through facial expression, physical comfort, or offers of an object
  • Begins to use words to express emotional states before or during emotional escalation
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Name emotions constantly during caregiving: 'You are crying. You are sad. I am here'
  • 2Read books with emotional content and name the characters' feelings explicitly
  • 3Mirror emotional expressions back to infants: when they smile, smile; when they frown with concern, show concern
  • 4Brackett (2019): emotion vocabulary is a learnable skill; toddlers with 15 or more emotion words show significantly better social competence
SE.2.a Develops early capacity for emotional regulation with caregiver support. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Calms in response to consistent caregiver soothing strategies
  • Uses a comfort object or self-soothing behavior for brief periods
  • Shows increasing predictability in distress and recovery patterns
  • Responds to caregiver co-regulation with decreased arousal
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Uses simple regulation strategies with caregiver coaching: deep breath, going to a quiet space, using a comfort object
  • Accepts caregiver redirection and co-regulation most of the time
  • Shows growing ability to recover from emotional upset with caregiver support
  • Begins to signal distress before full escalation in familiar, safe environments
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Co-regulation precedes self-regulation; toddlers borrow the caregiver's regulated nervous system before they develop their own
  • 2Respond to distress before it escalates; early intervention is more effective and less disruptive than crisis management
  • 3Avoid time-out, isolation, or withdrawal of warmth as responses to emotional dysregulation; these increase distress and undermine trust
  • 4Create calm-down spaces with soft materials, low light, and a comfort object; these are not punitive spaces but regulatory resources
SE.3.a Develops early empathy and awareness of others. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Shows concern when another infant cries
  • Imitates the emotional expressions of a familiar caregiver
  • Responds to distress in others with changed attention and expression
  • Initiates affectionate contact with familiar people
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Shows concern and attempts comfort when a peer is hurt or upset
  • Notices and names emotions in familiar peers
  • Offers a toy or comfort object to a distressed peer occasionally
  • Begins to consider others' feelings with adult prompting
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Model empathy explicitly and narrate it: 'Mia is crying. She is sad. Let us go check on her. What could we do?'
  • 2Never force affection (hugs, kisses); modeling is more powerful than coercion and preserves body autonomy
  • 3Eisenberg et al. (2015): early empathy is a predictor of prosocial behavior, academic success, and mental health across development
  • 4Read books with characters who have varied emotional experiences; this builds the emotional perspective-taking capacity that empathy requires
SE.4.a Develops secure, trusting relationships with consistent caregivers. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Shows clear preference for primary caregiver by two to three months
  • Uses primary caregiver as a secure base for exploration
  • Seeks primary caregiver for comfort when distressed
  • Responds to caregiver initiations with engagement and delight
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Seeks familiar caregivers for comfort, play, and reassurance
  • Greets familiar caregivers warmly after separation
  • Uses a trusted caregiver as a resource for problem-solving and exploration
  • Shows distress at caregiver departure and welcomes their return
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Primary caregiving assignment is not a nice-to-have; it is the structural prerequisite for secure attachment in group care
  • 2Invest in transition rituals: consistent hello and goodbye routines build predictability and reduce separation distress
  • 3Pianta et al. (2008): the teacher-child relationship quality is the strongest predictor of social-emotional and academic outcomes in early childhood
  • 4Repair matters more than perfection; when a caregiver makes a mistake (is impatient, misses a cue), the repair strengthens rather than weakens the relationship
SE.4.b Develops beginning social competence with familiar peers. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Shows interest in the faces and vocalizations of other infants
  • Initiates brief interaction with familiar peers
  • Engages in simple back-and-forth interaction with a peer with adult facilitation
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Plays alongside peers with awareness of what they are doing (parallel play)
  • Initiates brief interactions with peers through gestures and words
  • Shares a toy or material with adult prompting
  • Begins to prefer certain peers for play and seeks them out
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Parallel play is developmentally appropriate and sophisticated; resist pushing toddlers toward cooperative play before they are ready
  • 2Facilitate rather than direct peer interactions: stay nearby, narrate, and support without managing
  • 3Provide duplicate materials; toddlers do not yet have the regulatory capacity to share reliably and should not be expected to
  • 4CASEL research: social competence interventions in toddlerhood produce measurable benefits to prosocial behavior and school readiness
SE.4.c Develops beginning strategies for navigating conflict. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Protests by crying or pulling away when a toy is taken
  • Uses gestures to communicate displeasure
  • Accepts caregiver mediation with brief soothing
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Uses words to communicate displeasure: 'Mine!', 'No!', 'Stop!'
  • Accepts caregiver mediation in most peer conflicts
  • Occasionally uses a simple negotiation strategy: 'I had it first,' 'Can I have it after?'
  • Moves away from a conflict rather than escalating it in some situations
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Narrate conflicts from both sides: 'Maya had the truck first. Ben wants a turn. Both of you want it. What can we do?'
  • 2Teach simple conflict language and practice it when children are calm, not mid-conflict
  • 3Do not punish conflict behavior; toddler conflict is a social skill in formation, not a character problem
  • 4Shure (2001): children who learn basic problem-solving steps in toddlerhood show lower rates of aggression and higher rates of prosocial behavior through adolescence
SS

Social Studies

Infant and Toddler Track · Birth through 36 Months · 4 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Social Studies in the first three years is the curriculum of belonging, identity, community, and place. Infants and toddlers are not too young for social studies; they are the perfect age for it. Questions of who am I, who loves me, where do I belong, and what is the world like are not abstract philosophical inquiries for young children; they are the urgent developmental questions of the first three years, answered through daily experience in the caregiving relationship, the classroom community, and the wider world. Howard Gardner's interpersonal and intrapersonal intelligences are most visibly exercised in this domain: the infant who learns to read a caregiver's emotional face is developing interpersonal intelligence; the toddler who says 'I am Maya' is developing intrapersonal intelligence. Anti-bias education principles (Derman-Sparks and Edwards, 2010) are not preschool concepts; they begin in infancy. The images on the walls, the books on the shelf, the music in the air, and the languages spoken in the room all communicate to every infant and toddler whether or not this is a world made for them. Trauma-informed social studies recognizes that concepts of family, home, community helpers, and safety may be complicated or painful for children who have experienced family disruption, housing instability, or unsafe environments. Approach these concepts with flexibility, honesty, and care.

SoR
SoR connection: Social studies builds the world knowledge and cultural knowledge that are central to reading comprehension. A toddler who has heard family stories, celebrated cultural traditions, and visited community places has the experiential schema that will make social studies texts comprehensible years later.
SS.1.a Develops awareness of own identity and the diversity of people and families. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Recognizes and responds to family members and other familiar caregivers
  • Shows recognition of cultural foods, music, and objects from the home environment
  • Responds positively to images of people who look like family members
  • Attends to human faces with particular intensity from birth
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Names family members and describes basic roles: mama feeds me, daddy gives baths
  • Identifies self as belonging to a specific family and community
  • Shows interest in and curiosity about cultural differences in food, language, and customs
  • Uses home language words and shows comfort and pride in home culture
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Display photographs of every child's family at the child's eye level from the first day of care
  • 2Learn and use home language greetings, terms of endearment, and comfort words for every family in the room
  • 3Derman-Sparks and Edwards (2010): children notice race, gender, and ability in infancy; an environment that reflects their world is not optional
  • 4Books, music, and materials should authentically represent the families served, not a sanitized version of diversity
SS.1.b Develops early understanding of needs, wants, and community roles. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Communicates needs through crying, gesture, and later vocalization
  • Distinguishes between caregiving contexts (feeding, play, comfort) and responds accordingly
  • Shows satisfaction when needs are met and distress when they are not
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Identifies basic needs: food, water, sleep, love, and safety
  • Distinguishes between something needed and something wanted with adult support
  • Identifies one or two community helpers by name and role
  • Connects the role of familiar helpers to meeting needs: 'The doctor helps when you are sick'
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Meet needs promptly and consistently; this is needs-and-wants curriculum delivered through experience, not instruction
  • 2Name the difference between needs and wants during everyday moments: 'You need your coat; it is cold. You want the red one. Let us get the red coat'
  • 3Community walks to observe helpers in context: mail carriers, crossing guards, nurses, grocery workers
  • 4NCSS (2019): economic concepts including scarcity, choice, and interdependence begin with toddler experience of needs and wants
SS.1.c Develops beginning understanding of rules, rights, and shared community. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Responds to consistent caregiver expectations with increasing cooperation
  • Follows simple safety routines with consistent adult cuing
  • Shows awareness of classroom structures and expectations through anticipation of routine
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Follows simple classroom rules with adult reminders
  • Participates in classroom cleanup and care routines with adult guidance
  • Begins to understand that rules help the group stay safe and happy
  • Shows awareness when rules are broken and may communicate about it
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Keep rules simple, positive, and consistent: 'Gentle hands,' 'Walking feet inside,' 'We share the materials'
  • 2Explain the reason behind every rule in simple terms; toddlers follow rules better when they understand why
  • 3Involve toddlers in cleanup routines; this builds responsibility and community belonging simultaneously
  • 4Classroom community begins with the first day; routines and roles make belonging visible and real
SS.1.d Develops beginning ability to care for materials and the environment. ⌄
Infant — Birth to 12 Months
  • Tolerates gentle guidance in handling materials
  • Responds to caregiver modeling of careful material handling
  • Shows interest in observing the caregiver care for the room
Toddler — 12 to 36 Months
  • Returns materials to the shelf with adult reminders and physical guidance
  • Handles books and materials with increasing care
  • Participates in classroom cleanup with enthusiastic adult participation
  • Notices when materials are broken or misused and communicates about it
Caregiver Strategies
  • 1Montessori's foundational insight applies fully here: materials that are beautiful, complete, and organized invite care
  • 2Clean up together every time, with genuine engagement rather than supervision; toddlers do not learn care by being directed to it
  • 3Living things in the classroom (plants, fish, classroom garden) build care and stewardship more powerfully than any lesson
  • 4Connect care of the classroom to care of the community: 'We take care of our room the same way neighbors take care of their street'
