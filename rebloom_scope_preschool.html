Curriculum Philosophy and Research Base

re:Bloom is a research-grounded, play-based early childhood curriculum designed for licensed child care programs serving children birth through age five. The Preschool Scope and Sequence covers children ages 3–5 across all nine developmental domains, with full integration of Science of Reading principles, trauma-informed practices, and inclusive frameworks throughout. The curriculum is built to be implemented by educators at all experience levels and is designed to support program quality goals including QRIS documentation, CLASS observation alignment, and kindergarten readiness.

The theoretical foundation of re:Bloom draws on the most influential and research-supported frameworks in child development and education. Jean Piaget's constructivism grounds our understanding that children build knowledge through active, hands-on interaction with materials and ideas, not through passive absorption of adult-delivered content. Lev Vygotsky's sociocultural theory shapes our emphasis on rich language interaction, collaborative play, and scaffolded instruction within each child's Zone of Proximal Development. Maria Montessori's principles of the prepared environment, intrinsic motivation, and child-directed work inform our physical environment design and center-time philosophy. Howard Gardner's multiple intelligences framework ensures that our curriculum honors diverse learning pathways. The Harvard Center on the Developing Child's science of learning and development provides the neurobiological grounding for our emphasis on relationships, executive function, and stress regulation as prerequisites for academic learning.

Science of Reading (SoR) principles are woven throughout all nine domains, not only Language and Literacy. Reading is a complex skill built on oral language, phonological awareness, alphabetic knowledge, and language comprehension, each of which is developed across the full curriculum. Physical development supports handwriting fluency. Social-emotional development supports the regulatory attention that learning to read requires. Cognitive development provides the symbolic thinking that the alphabetic principle demands. Science and Social Studies build the background knowledge that reading comprehension depends on. re:Bloom treats the entire curriculum as literacy preparation.

Trauma-informed care (TIC) principles are embedded throughout this curriculum. Children who have experienced adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) or chronic stress arrive in our classrooms with neurological and behavioral adaptations that require understanding rather than correction. re:Bloom educators maintain predictable routines, build genuine relationships, interpret behavior through a trauma lens, and use co-regulation before expecting self-regulation. Inclusion principles are similarly embedded: this curriculum is designed to be implemented with and for all children, including those with individualized education or family service plans, language differences, developmental delays, and other forms of diversity. Adaptation, accommodation, and full belonging are not addenda to re:Bloom; they are its foundation.

Daily Schedule Framework

The following schedule framework reflects the re:Bloom Play-Based Daily Structure for preschool classrooms. It is a framework, not a rigid prescription; programs should adapt timing to meet licensing requirements, family schedules, and child needs. The non-negotiables are: uninterrupted child-directed play of at least 60 minutes, daily read-aloud with interactive comprehension support, daily phonological awareness instruction, and daily outdoor time of at least 45 minutes.

Closing Meeting / Reflection Cycle SE, LL, SS, AL Day recall and reflection, community closing ritual, family communication, preview of tomorrow. Children revisit their work, name what they made or did, and connect intention to outcome — building metacognitive habits from the earliest years.

Assessment Overview

The re:Bloom assessment system is designed to be embedded in authentic classroom practice rather than conducted as separate testing events. Assessment in a play-based preschool program is primarily observational, documentation-based, and ongoing. The following components comprise the full re:Bloom Preschool Assessment System:

Child Observation and Assessment Tool

The re:Bloom Child Observation and Assessment Tool covers all nine domains with 44 standards and age-differentiated indicators. It uses a four-level rating scale: Not Yet Observed, Emerging, Developing, and Consistently Observed. The tool is fill-and-print, generates an AI-written developmental summary and next steps, and is designed for use three times per year (fall, winter, spring) for a complete developmental portrait. Detailed instructions for administration, scoring, and use in QRIS documentation are contained in the re:Bloom Assessment Guide.

Anecdotal Records and Running Notes

Educators take brief anecdotal notes during observation of child-directed play and small group instruction. A minimum of 2 to 3 anecdotes per child per week across the group is the expectation. Anecdotes are filed by domain code and child, and inform the formal assessment tool at each rating period. The re:Bloom Assessment Guide includes a format template.

Portfolio Documentation

Each child's portfolio includes: dated work samples across all nine domains, photographs with educator notations, and the completed assessment tool at each rating period. Portfolios are shared with families at scheduled conferences and form the basis of family-facing progress summaries. Portfolio documentation directly supports QRIS requirements for documentation of individualized planning.

AL

Approaches to Learning

Preschool Track · Ages 3 to 5 · 4 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Approaches to Learning encompasses the dispositions, behaviors, and habits of mind that shape how children engage with learning experiences. Research consistently identifies initiative, curiosity, persistence, creativity, and self-regulation as stronger predictors of school success than specific academic content knowledge (Duncan et al., 2007). Vygotsky's concept of the Zone of Proximal Development underpins this domain: children grow most when supported at the edge of their current capacity. Howard Gardner's work on multiple intelligences reminds us that children approach learning through diverse pathways: linguistic, logical, musical, spatial, bodily, interpersonal, intrapersonal, and naturalistic. Effective early childhood programs cultivate all of them. Trauma-informed practice is essential in this domain: children whose early experiences include chronic stress or adversity may exhibit reduced risk-taking and persistence not as deficits but as adaptive survival responses. Meeting these children where they are, building safety, and celebrating incremental growth are the entry points to rebuilding learning dispositions.

SoR
Science of Reading connection: Approaches to Learning dispositions, particularly persistence, attention regulation, and willingness to take risks, directly support phonological awareness instruction and early decoding. Children who maintain engagement during challenging tasks (like blending phonemes or segmenting words) develop reading skills faster. Build AL simultaneously with literacy instruction.
AL.1.a Engages in new and unfamiliar experiences and activities. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Approaches new materials with curiosity and interest
  • Tries new activities without requiring significant adult persuasion
  • Tolerates ambiguity and uncertainty in open-ended tasks
  • Verbally expresses interest in what something is or how it works
Educator Strategies
  • 1Introduce one new material or provocation per week; allow repeated exploration before rotating
  • 2Use Montessori-inspired 'invitations to explore': arrange intriguing materials without instructions
  • 3Model curiosity aloud: 'I wonder what would happen if...' to normalize not-knowing
  • 4Acknowledge the discomfort of novelty before encouraging engagement: 'New things can feel strange at first'
AL.1.b Completes activities with increasingly complex steps. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Follows three or more step directions to complete a project
  • Plans a sequence of actions before beginning
  • Returns to multi-step projects over multiple days
  • Adjusts plan when a step does not work as expected
Educator Strategies
  • 1Use intentional play cycles daily at center time: children choose their work, carry it out, and reflect on what they did
  • 2Provide materials that require multi-step processes (cooking, building, art projects)
  • 3Explicitly narrate sequencing: 'First we did... then we... what comes next?'
  • 4Avoid completing tasks for children; Vygotsky's scaffolding means supporting without replacing agency
AL.1.c Persists in completing a task with increasing concentration. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Sustains engagement in a self-chosen task for 15 or more minutes
  • Resists distraction during a preferred activity
  • Tries a different approach when first attempt fails
  • Expresses satisfaction or pride when completing something difficult
Educator Strategies
  • 1Honor long periods of deep play; do not interrupt for transitions unnecessarily
  • 2Offer process-based encouragement rather than product praise: 'You kept trying even when it was hard'
  • 3Use Dweck's growth mindset language throughout the day: 'Not yet' instead of 'You can't'
  • 4Document and revisit children's work to build a sense of continuity and purpose
AL.2.a Develops a growth mindset and learning orientation. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Views mistakes as expected and useful parts of learning
  • Welcomes challenge rather than avoiding it
  • Notices and names own growing abilities
  • Encourages peers when they are struggling
Educator Strategies
  • 1Read aloud books with growth mindset themes and explicitly connect them to classroom moments
  • 2Maintain a 'Wall of Learning' showing work in progress, not just finished products
  • 3Avoid labeling children as 'smart'; research (Dweck, 2006) shows this undermines persistence
  • 4Normalize the word 'yet': 'You haven't figured this out yet. That means your brain is working'
CO

Cognitive Development

Preschool Track · Ages 3 to 5 · 4 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Cognitive development in the preschool years is characterized by rapid growth in memory, symbolic thinking, problem-solving, and cognitive flexibility. Jean Piaget's framework describes preschoolers as pre-operational thinkers who learn through active, hands-on manipulation of objects and ideas rather than through abstract instruction. They are beginning to decenter, meaning they consider perspectives beyond their own, and to use mental representations as the foundation of literacy, mathematics, and scientific reasoning. Lev Vygotsky contributed the critical understanding that cognitive development is inherently social: higher-order thinking emerges first between children (intermental) before it becomes internal (intramental). This means that rich conversation, collaborative play, and adult scaffolding are not supplements to cognitive development; they are its engine. Executive function, the cluster of skills that includes working memory, cognitive flexibility, and inhibitory control, develops rapidly between ages 3 and 5 and is among the strongest predictors of academic and life outcomes (Diamond, 2013; Center on the Developing Child, 2020).

SoR
Science of Reading connection: Symbolic thinking (CO.2.a), the understanding that one thing can stand for another, is the cognitive foundation of the alphabetic principle. Children who develop strong symbolic thinking more readily grasp that letters represent sounds, which is the core insight that unlocks decoding.
CO.1.a Develops the ability to recall information about objects, people, and past experiences. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Accurately recalls events from previous days or weeks when prompted
  • Retells the sequence of a recent experience with correct ordering
  • Uses memory of past experiences to inform current decisions
  • Connects a current experience to a related memory without adult prompting
Educator Strategies
  • 1Use daily recall conversations: 'What did we do in the garden yesterday? What do you remember?'
  • 2Create documentation panels with photos and captions children help write
  • 3Build on prior knowledge explicitly: 'Remember when we tried to sink the foam block? What did we learn?'
  • 4Use Reggio-inspired learning stories that honor memory and narrative as cognitive tools
CO.2.a Demonstrates increasing ability to think symbolically. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Engages in complex pretend scenarios with multiple roles and storylines
  • Creates representations of real objects through drawing, building, or mark-making
  • Understands that print, numbers, and symbols carry meaning
  • Uses symbols and signs in play (makes signs for a restaurant, writes on a notepad)
Educator Strategies
  • 1Provide open-ended dramatic play materials that invite symbolic substitution (blocks as phones, fabric as capes)
  • 2Model symbol use throughout the environment: labeled shelves, charts, calendars, illustrated schedules
  • 3Connect symbolic play directly to reading: 'When you pretend the block is a car, you are thinking like a reader who knows letters are symbols'
  • 4Piaget's work supports child-directed symbolic play as the primary vehicle for this development; protect lengthy play blocks
CO.3.a Uses increasingly complex strategies to solve problems. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Generates two or more possible solutions before acting
  • Evaluates which approach worked and explains why
  • Applies a solution from one problem to a new, similar situation
  • Seeks information resources (books, teachers, peers) before giving up
Educator Strategies
  • 1Use open-ended provocations that have multiple valid solutions (engineering challenges, design problems)
  • 2Ask 'What are your ideas?' rather than 'What's the answer?'
  • 3Document children's problem-solving processes in writing to make thinking visible
  • 4Socratic questioning: 'What makes you think that? What else could be true?'
CO.4.a Develops ability to be flexible in own thinking and behavior. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Transitions between activities with minimal emotional disruption
  • Applies different rules in different contexts without adult reminders
  • Changes approach when presented with new information
  • Sustains attention and shifts attention flexibly as needed
Educator Strategies
  • 1Use visual schedules and transition warnings to support cognitive flexibility
  • 2Teach explicitly that 'rules are different here across settings: classroom, lunchroom, outdoor, and library
  • 3Play cognitive flexibility games (sorting the same objects by different attributes, following changing directions)
  • 4Harvard's research on executive function identifies flexibility as trainable; build it through routine game-based practice
CR

Creative Development

Preschool Track · Ages 3 to 5 · 3 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Creative development is not supplementary to academic readiness; it is foundational to it. Research consistently links arts engagement to language development, mathematical reasoning, social-emotional competence, and executive function (Education Commission of the States, 2021; Rood, Hadani, & Liberman, 2020). Howard Gardner's theory of multiple intelligences explicitly validates musical, spatial, and bodily-kinesthetic intelligences as distinct and significant learning pathways, not lesser versions of linguistic or logical intelligence. Maria Montessori's prepared environment principle applies powerfully here: beautiful, thoughtfully arranged art and music materials invite intrinsic creative engagement. Creative development in early childhood is characterized by process over product: the three-year-old who paints with complete absorption and the five-year-old who sings an original song are engaged in the same profound cognitive and emotional work regardless of the quality of the output.

SoR
Science of Reading connection: Creative Development supports phonological awareness through music and rhyme (CR.1.b). Singing, chanting, and rhythm work develop children's sensitivity to the sound structures of language, the same awareness that underlies phonemic awareness and early decoding.
CR.1.a Expresses ideas, feelings, and experiences through visual art. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Creates representations that are recognizable to others and connected to personal experience
  • Chooses specific colors, textures, and materials with clear intention
  • Describes own artwork and the process of creating it with vocabulary
  • Revisits and refines a piece across multiple sessions
Educator Strategies
  • 1Offer a consistent, beautiful, organized art studio area with rotating materials
  • 2Avoid coloring sheets and pre-made templates; these communicate that there is a right answer in art
  • 3Ask: 'Tell me about your artwork' rather than 'What is it?'; the child's meaning is the curriculum
  • 4Montessori's principle of beauty in the environment: art materials should be real, beautiful, and respected
CR.1.b Expresses self through music, movement, and dramatic play. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Sings songs with reasonably accurate words and melody
  • Creates original songs, chants, or rhythms spontaneously
  • Moves expressively to music with varied tempo and dynamics
  • Takes on roles in complex dramatic play scenarios
Educator Strategies
  • 1Integrate music into every routine: transitions, cleanup, gathering, meals; children learn language through song
  • 2Offer daily opportunities for unscripted dramatic play with open-ended materials
  • 3Use call-and-response and choral chanting; both support phonological awareness and oral language
  • 4Teach children to listen to music for emotional quality: 'What does this music make you feel?'
CR.2.a Develops ability to express original ideas through imaginative and inventive play. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Creates elaborate multi-day pretend scenarios with narrative arcs
  • Assigns roles, negotiates plot lines, and resolves in-play conflicts verbally
  • Invents solutions to in-play problems using available materials
  • Sustains imaginative play over extended time with increasing complexity
Educator Strategies
  • 1Block dramatic play interruptions; a child deep in a narrative is doing advanced cognitive and social work
  • 2Support play by adding materials that extend the story rather than redirecting it
  • 3Document play narratives through Vivian Paley's storytelling and story-acting approach
  • 4Connect in-play language to literacy: 'You just made up a story! Should we write it down?'
LL

Language & Literacy

Preschool Track · Ages 3 to 5 · 12 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Language and Literacy encompasses oral language, phonological awareness, phonics, vocabulary, fluency, comprehension, and writing, all of which are explicitly grounded in the Science of Reading. Hart and Risley's (1995) seminal research established that vocabulary exposure in the early years is among the most powerful predictors of reading achievement. Scarborough's Reading Rope (2001) models reading as the integration of word recognition (phonological awareness, decoding, sight recognition) and language comprehension (vocabulary, background knowledge, verbal reasoning, literacy knowledge). Both strands are developed simultaneously in a high-quality preschool environment. The Simple View of Reading (Gough & Tunmer, 1986), Reading = Decoding x Language Comprehension, grounds the SoR emphasis throughout this domain. Preschool is the optimal window for oral language development, phonological awareness, and print concept knowledge. Children who arrive at kindergarten with strong phonological awareness learn to read significantly more easily than those who do not, regardless of socioeconomic background (Kilpatrick, 2015).

SoR
This entire domain is grounded in Science of Reading research. LL.2.a–LL.2.c address phonological awareness directly. LL.1.a–LL.1.e develop language comprehension. LL.3.a–LL.3.b build print and alphabetic knowledge. LL.4.a–LL.4.b develop writing foundations. All four strands of Scarborough's Reading Rope are represented.
LL.1.a Demonstrates understanding of increasingly complex language. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Follows three or more step directions correctly
  • Understands comparative and superlative language (bigger, biggest, faster than)
  • Responds appropriately to conditional language ('if/then') in conversation
  • Understands spatial and temporal vocabulary accurately (before, after, between, behind)
Educator Strategies
  • 1Use precise, rich language throughout the day: not simplified speech but rather supported speech
  • 2Build spatial and temporal vocabulary deliberately through movement games and daily schedule discussions
  • 3Check for understanding through action rather than verbal confirmation; ask children to show rather than simply tell
  • 4Research (Biemiller & Slonim, 2001) supports explicit vocabulary instruction in preschool as high-impact
LL.1.b Develops and expands understanding of vocabulary and concepts. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Understands and uses Tier 2 vocabulary words introduced in instruction
  • Learns and retains 3–5 new words per week when explicitly taught
  • Explains word meanings in context using own language
  • Makes connections between new words and known words, concepts, or experiences
Educator Strategies
  • 1Use Beck, McKeown, and Kucan's Tier 2 vocabulary instruction framework; choose words that appear across many contexts
  • 2Teach words deeply: meaning, use in a sentence, connection to a concept, and a physical gesture
  • 3Revisit vocabulary words across the week in multiple contexts; a word taught once is rarely retained
  • 4Read-aloud books chosen partly for vocabulary richness: Biemiller's research supports this as highest-yield vocabulary instruction for preschoolers
LL.1.c Communicates using increasingly complex and grammatically sophisticated language. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Uses complete sentences of 5–6 or more words consistently
  • Uses compound and complex sentence structures in conversation
  • Uses language to explain reasoning, describe cause and effect, and make comparisons
  • Speech is fully intelligible to unfamiliar adults in most contexts
Educator Strategies
  • 1Expand and extend children's utterances naturally: if a child says 'dog ran,' respond 'Yes, the big dog ran very fast down the hill!'
  • 2Use Dialogic Reading techniques during read-alouds: PEER (Prompt, Evaluate, Expand, Repeat)
  • 3Avoid correcting grammar directly; model correct form in your response
  • 4Create a culture of talking: table conversations, partner share, whole-group discussion, and one-on-one conversation during play
LL.1.d Participates in conversations using turn-taking, topic maintenance, and listening skills. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Sustains a topic across 3 or more conversational turns
  • Listens without interrupting during structured discussion (with support)
  • Adjusts language and tone for different listeners
  • Asks clarifying questions to extend or deepen conversation
Educator Strategies
  • 1Teach turn-taking explicitly using visual tools (talking stick, conversation cards)
  • 2Use accountable talk structures: 'I agree with ___ because...'; 'I want to add...'
  • 3Model active listening visibly and narrate it: 'I'm going to look at you and wait because I want to hear your whole idea'
  • 4Conversation is the primary vehicle for oral language development; protect and prioritize it over adult-directed instruction
LL.1.e Develops comprehension of read-aloud text across literary and informational genres. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Retells main events in sequence after a read-aloud
  • Answers both literal and inferential questions about text
  • Makes predictions before and during reading using text evidence
  • Makes connections between texts and personal experience, other books, or world knowledge
Educator Strategies
  • 1Use Interactive Read-Aloud methodology: stop for thinking, prediction, visualization, and connection
  • 2Balance literary and informational text; informational texts build background knowledge, a critical reading comprehension variable
  • 3Before reading: activate background knowledge. During reading: model thinking aloud. After reading: extend meaning through drawing, drama, or discussion
  • 4Research (Nation & Snowling, 2004): comprehension strategy instruction in preschool has lasting effects on reading comprehension in elementary school
LL.2.a Develops awareness of the syllable structure of spoken words. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Claps or taps syllables in multi-syllabic words accurately
  • Identifies whether a word has one, two, or more syllables
  • Blends two syllables to form a compound word
  • Segments two-syllable words into component parts
Educator Strategies
  • 1Syllable clapping is an entry point for phonological awareness; use it daily in transitions, songs, and name games
  • 2Work at the syllable level before moving to onset-rime or phoneme levels; this is the phonological awareness hierarchy
  • 3Use children's names as primary teaching material: 'Ma-ri-a has three syllables; how many does your name have?'
  • 4Kilpatrick (2015): syllable awareness typically precedes and supports phoneme awareness development
LL.2.b Develops awareness of initial sounds, onsets, and rime patterns. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Identifies words that begin with the same sound
  • Isolates the initial consonant sound in a familiar one-syllable word
  • Blends an onset and rime to form a recognizable word
  • Generates words that begin with a given target sound
Educator Strategies
  • 1Alliteration tongue twisters, alphabet books, and sound-sorting games develop onset awareness
  • 2Use Elkonin boxes (sound boxes) to make onset-rime segmentation concrete and visual
  • 3Initial sound identification is a critical precursor to phoneme-level awareness; do not rush past it
  • 4Match phonological awareness instruction to books being read aloud: find words with target sounds in current texts
LL.2.c Develops understanding and production of rhyme. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Identifies whether two words rhyme with high accuracy
  • Generates multiple rhyming words for a given word
  • Distinguishes rhyming from non-rhyming word pairs
  • Appreciates and creates playful rhymes independently
Educator Strategies
  • 1Rhyme is among the first phonological awareness skills to emerge; build it through daily songs, nursery rhymes, and books
  • 2Teach the difference between 'sounds the same at the end' (rhyme) and 'sounds the same at the beginning' (alliteration)
  • 3When reading rhyming books, pause before the rhyming word and allow children to produce it
  • 4Research confirms: children who can produce rhymes easily have stronger phoneme awareness and decoding foundations (Bryant et al., 1990)
LL.3.a Develops knowledge of print organization and the functions of print. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Understands print is read left to right and top to bottom in English
  • Identifies a word, letter, sentence, and page in a text
  • Locates specific words or letters in familiar environmental print
  • Distinguishes between print and pictures on a page
Educator Strategies
  • 1Point to text while reading aloud; this simple act builds enormous print concept knowledge over time
  • 2Use shared writing experiences daily: children dictate, teacher writes, pointing to each word
  • 3Create a print-rich environment where labels, signs, charts, and lists serve real communicative purposes
  • 4Concepts About Print (Clay, 2000) assessment items directly inform which skills to target next
LL.3.b Develops knowledge of the alphabet and the alphabetic principle. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Names and identifies most uppercase and 10 or more lowercase letters
  • Produces the most common sound for several letters
  • Identifies the letter that represents a given initial sound in familiar words
  • Writes several letters independently including own first name
Educator Strategies
  • 1Teach letters in an order informed by frequency and utility, not A-B-C order, to accelerate decoding access
  • 2Use multisensory letter practice: sand writing, sky writing, playdough letters, tactile letter cards
  • 3Connect letter learning immediately to phoneme awareness: teaching 'B says /b/' alongside words that begin with /b/
  • 4Explicit, systematic alphabetic knowledge instruction is supported by the full body of SoR research (Adams, 1990; Kilpatrick, 2015)
LL.4.a Develops understanding that writing represents spoken language. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Understands that letters and words on a page represent spoken words
  • Asks adults to read back what they wrote
  • Attempts to write words by saying them aloud and writing letters for sounds
  • Writes own name to communicate identity and ownership
Educator Strategies
  • 1Daily interactive writing practice: children and teacher compose together, sharing the pen
  • 2Name writing is both literacy instruction and identity development; teach it with full intentionality
  • 3Connect writing to reading: 'You just read that word with your eyes; now try to write it with your hand'
  • 4Writing attempts at this age should be celebrated rather than corrected; developmental spelling is a literacy behavior, not an error
LL.4.b Draws and writes using an increasingly controlled and efficient grip and letter formation. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Uses a dynamic tripod or similar mature grasp for writing tools
  • Copies basic geometric shapes with accuracy
  • Produces recognizable letters with controlled directionality
  • Writes from left to right with increasing consistency
Educator Strategies
  • 1Strengthen hand muscles through daily fine motor work: tongs, lacing, playdough, cutting; playdough, cutting, and building are more effective
  • 2Teach correct pencil grip explicitly and early; inefficient grips become habits that are difficult to change
  • 3Handwriting is a readiness skill for fluent writing; efficient grip reduces cognitive load and supports encoding
  • 4Provide a variety of writing tools: thin markers, crayons, and pencils; let children develop a tool preference while maintaining technique
MA

Mathematics

Preschool Track · Ages 3 to 5 · 9 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Mathematics in early childhood is not computation practice but the development of mathematical thinking, reasoning, and disposition. Clements and Sarama's (2014) learning trajectories framework provides the research foundation for the re:Bloom mathematics sequence: children develop mathematical understanding along predictable but not rigid pathways, and instruction is most effective when it meets children at their current level and moves them forward through progressively complex experiences. Piaget's concrete-representational-abstract progression is foundational here: preschool mathematics is primarily concrete, with abstract symbol introduction supported by extensive physical manipulation. The NCTM (2006) Curriculum Focal Points identify number, operations, and geometry as the core foci for preschool. Executive function, particularly working memory and cognitive flexibility, is deeply implicated in mathematical reasoning and responds to the same environmental supports that benefit all executive function development.

SoR
Science of Reading connection: Number word knowledge (MA.1.a) develops through the same oral language pathways as vocabulary. Counting rhymes, number songs, and mathematical vocabulary instruction directly reinforce the phonological awareness and vocabulary knowledge that support reading.
MA.1.a Develops understanding of the counting sequence and its stable order. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Counts accurately to 20 without skipping or repeating numbers
  • Counts backward from 10 with accuracy
  • Begins counting a set from any number, not always from 1
  • Counts by 10s to 100 with support
Educator Strategies
  • 1Count everything, everywhere: steps, bites, children, crackers; counting is not math time, it is all day
  • 2Counting songs and chants build the stable-order principle through repetition and rhythm
  • 3When children make counting errors, note the specific error: skipping, repeating, or losing track; different errors require different support
  • 4Clements and Sarama: the stable order of counting words is a conceptual achievement that underlies all counting with correspondence
MA.1.b Develops understanding of one-to-one correspondence and cardinality. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Counts a set of up to 10 objects with reliable one-to-one correspondence
  • Understands that the last number stated equals the total (cardinality principle)
  • Counts out a specific number from a larger set accurately
  • Compares two sets and determines 'more,' 'less,' or 'equal'
Educator Strategies
  • 1Provide objects to count during every table routine: crackers, napkins, cups, manipulatives
  • 2Use explicit cardinality language: 'You counted 7, so there are 7 bears. The last number we count tells us how many.'
  • 3Subitizing practice (dot cards, dice) develops cardinality intuition; begin with sets of 2 and build to 5
  • 4Gelman and Gallistel's five counting principles provide the research map for cardinality development
MA.1.c Develops the ability to subitize small quantities. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Instantly recognizes groups of 1–3 without counting
  • Recognizes groups of 4–5 with brief exposure and practice
  • Names the quantity of a small group without counting aloud
  • Uses subitizing to compare small groups quickly
Educator Strategies
  • 1Daily dot card flash activities: 2 seconds of exposure, then 'How many did you see?'
  • 2Connect subitizing to dice use in board games: natural, frequent, meaningful
  • 3Distinguish between perceptual subitizing (1–3, instant) and conceptual subitizing (4–8, chunked)
  • 4Clements (1999): subitizing development predicts later arithmetic fluency; invest in it daily in preschool
MA.2.a Develops understanding of number relationships and early operations. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Combines two groups of objects and counts the total (addition)
  • Removes objects from a group and counts what remains (subtraction)
  • Solves simple addition and subtraction story problems using objects
  • Uses number relationships to compare quantities (5 is more than 3 because...)
Educator Strategies
  • 1Story problems embedded in dramatic play are the most authentic entry point for operations: 'We had 4 bears and 2 left. How many are still here?'
  • 2Use five-frames and ten-frames as visual structures for part-whole thinking
  • 3Avoid introducing written number sentences before children have extensive concrete experience
  • 4Research shows preschoolers can solve problems using sophisticated informal strategies long before formal instruction; trust and facilitate this capacity
MA.3.a Develops knowledge of measurement concepts. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Compares and orders 3 or more objects by length, weight, or capacity
  • Uses non-standard units to measure with consistency
  • Uses accurate measurement vocabulary (longer, shorter, heavier, lighter, holds more)
  • Understands that measuring requires a consistent unit
Educator Strategies
  • 1Cooking is the richest measurement curriculum available; use it weekly
  • 2Measure everything: height with blocks, weight on a balance scale, and capacity with sand and water
  • 3Introduce non-standard measurement before standard units; this builds the conceptual understanding that formal measurement later formalizes
  • 4Clements and Sarama: measurement understanding at preschool level predicts mathematics achievement through grade 5
MA.3.b Develops the ability to sort, classify, and understand attributes. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Sorts a collection by a single attribute with accuracy
  • Sorts the same collection by a different attribute when asked
  • Names the sorting rule before or after sorting
  • Creates own categories and explains the reasoning behind them
Educator Strategies
  • 1Provide collections for sorting: buttons, shells, leaves, lids, blocks; varied attributes invite varied sorting rules
  • 2Ask 'How did you sort these?' before 'Why?' because children need to describe before they can explain
  • 3Introduce attribute blocks as a mathematically rich sorting material
  • 4Sorting is pre-algebraic thinking: the child who sorts by color and then by size is developing the pattern recognition that underlies algebraic reasoning
MA.3.c Develops understanding of patterns and their structure. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Creates and extends AB and ABB patterns independently
  • Identifies the repeating unit in a pattern
  • Creates patterns with three or more elements
  • Translates a pattern across different representations (color → sound → movement)
Educator Strategies
  • 1Pattern work should move from physical to visual to symbolic: clap patterns, then block patterns, then color patterns
  • 2Teach children to identify the core unit: 'What part repeats?' is the essential pattern question
  • 3Pattern translation (same pattern in different materials) develops abstract pattern understanding
  • 4NCTM: algebraic thinking begins with pattern recognition in preschool; invest in it as mathematical literacy
MA.4.a Develops ability to recognize and describe shapes and their attributes. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Names circle, square, triangle, rectangle, oval, and hexagon reliably
  • Describes shapes by number of sides and corners
  • Identifies shapes in different orientations and sizes
  • Names common 3D shapes (sphere, cube, cylinder, cone)
Educator Strategies
  • 1Shapes must be presented in varied orientations from the start; a tilted square is still a square
  • 2Go on shape walks: find rectangles in windows, circles in wheels, triangles in rooftops
  • 3Use geoboards and tangrams for active shape composition and decomposition
  • 4Van Hiele's levels of geometric thinking: preschoolers are at Level 0 (visual) moving toward Level 1 (analysis); attribute description is the bridge
MA.4.b Develops understanding of spatial relationships. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Uses and understands spatial language accurately (in front of, behind, between, beside, above, below)
  • Creates and follows a simple diagram or map of a familiar space
  • Completes 12+ piece puzzles
  • Describes the location of objects using accurate positional language
Educator Strategies
  • 1Spatial language instruction has outsized impact on mathematical development; use it constantly and precisely
  • 2Building blocks support spatial reasoning more than almost any other material; protect and extend block play
  • 3Body movement games that use spatial vocabulary: 'Stand beside the door, then move behind the chair'
  • 4Research (Verdine et al., 2014): spatial skills at age 3 predict mathematics achievement at age 5, regardless of other factors
PW

Physical Development & Wellness

Preschool Track · Ages 3 to 5 · 9 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Physical Development encompasses both the motor competencies and wellness practices that support children's long-term health, school readiness, and quality of life. Gross motor development in preschool, including running, jumping, climbing, and balancing, builds the proprioceptive and vestibular processing that supports seated attention and fine motor control. Fine motor development, including gripping, manipulating, cutting, and writing, is the embodied foundation of literacy and mathematics tool use. Sensory processing (PW.1.d) has received increased attention in the research literature: children who have difficulty regulating sensory input may exhibit behaviors that are frequently misread as behavioral problems rather than neurological differences requiring environmental accommodation. Trauma-informed care is critical in this domain: physical boundaries, body autonomy, and safe touch are not just wellness concepts but trauma recovery and prevention practices. Inclusion considerations: children with physical disabilities or atypical motor development are entitled to full participation in physical experiences through individualized modifications and adaptive tools.

SoR
Science of Reading connection: Fine motor control (PW.1.b) directly supports efficient handwriting, which research (Berninger, 2012) shows is connected to writing fluency and composition, itself connected to reading achievement through shared linguistic pathways.
PW.1.a Develops competency in a variety of locomotor and non-locomotor skills. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Runs with control of speed and direction
  • Hops on one foot 3 or more consecutive times
  • Skips with an alternating step-hop pattern
  • Walks up and down stairs alternating feet
  • Throws, catches, and kicks with increasing coordination and accuracy
Educator Strategies
  • 1Intentional gross motor curriculum is not extra but essential neurological development
  • 2Obstacle courses, movement games, and structured outdoor play each address multiple locomotor skills simultaneously
  • 3Gallahue and Ozmun (2012): the fundamental movement phase (ages 2–7) is the window for locomotor skill development; missing it has lasting consequences
  • 4Avoid over-restricting outdoor movement for safety; calculated risk in physical play builds proprioception, confidence, and competence
PW.1.b Demonstrates developing control and precision in fine motor skills. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Cuts along a line and around simple shapes with scissors
  • Strings small beads or laces with control
  • Completes 20+ piece puzzles independently
  • Demonstrates tripod grasp for writing tools with stability
Educator Strategies
  • 1Fine motor development requires daily practice embedded in centers: tongs at the sensory table, tweezers in science, lacing in the manipulatives area
  • 2Avoid premature pencil work as the primary fine motor vehicle; playdough, cutting, and building are more effective
  • 3Provide real tools that work: child-safe scissors that actually cut, tongs that grip, and lacing boards with laces that hold
  • 4Handwriting Without Tears research supports specific stroke sequences and grip instruction before age 5
PW.1.c Develops oral motor skills in service of articulation and self-care. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Produces most English speech sounds accurately for age
  • Uses utensils for eating with control and appropriate grip
  • Manages a variety of food textures without difficulty
  • Blows, sucks, and uses tongue movements for age-appropriate activities
Educator Strategies
  • 1Refer concerns about speech sound production to a speech-language pathologist; oral motor development affects academic outcomes
  • 2Mealtime is oral motor curriculum: offer textures, temperatures, and tools that require varied oral motor work
  • 3Blowing instruments such as harmonicas, whistles, and pinwheels support oral motor strength and awareness
  • 4ASHA developmental norms indicate most consonants should be fully acquired by age 8, with many in place by age 4
PW.1.d Uses senses to guide movement and engagement with the environment. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Adjusts movement in response to sensory information (steps around obstacles, catches a rolling ball)
  • Manages varied sensory environments without significant dysregulation
  • Describes sensory experiences using vocabulary for texture, temperature, sound, and movement
  • Shows appropriate sensory seeking or avoidance preferences
Educator Strategies
  • 1Create environments that are both sensory-rich and sensory-predictable; children need stimulation and sanctuary in equal measure
  • 2Offer sensory alternatives; a child who needs movement during circle time may learn better on a wobble seat or while standing
  • 3Sensory processing differences are neurological rather than behavioral; accommodation is inclusion, not permissiveness
  • 4Ayres Sensory Integration theory: sensory processing is foundational to all motor, cognitive, and social learning
PW.2.a Develops knowledge of the body, its parts, functions, and needs. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Names and locates all major body parts including internal organs
  • Describes basic functions of the brain, heart, and lungs in child-accessible terms
  • Understands that the body needs nutritious food, water, sleep, and movement
  • Connects physical feelings (hunger, fatigue, nervousness) to body signals
Educator Strategies
  • 1Use body-focused read-alouds and informational books to build body content knowledge
  • 2Create a classroom 'body inquiry': chart what we know and what we wonder about how bodies work
  • 3Interoception, awareness of internal body states, is a learnable skill (Craig, 2015) that supports both health and emotional regulation
  • 4Connect physical wellness to emotional wellness explicitly; for example: 'When your body feels rested, it is easier to be patient'
PW.2.b Demonstrates personal health and self-care practices with increasing independence. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Washes hands correctly and independently following all steps
  • Dresses and undresses independently, managing buttons and zippers with effort
  • Brushes teeth and blows/wipes nose independently
  • Manages toileting completely independently
Educator Strategies
  • 1Post visual handwashing steps at child height; the visual cue supports independence
  • 2Self-care is both independence-building and fine motor curriculum; invest time in teaching it rather than simply managing it
  • 3Derman-Sparks & Edwards (2010): self-care competence contributes to positive self-identity and cultural respect
  • 4Acknowledge cultural variation in self-care practices; home routines may differ from school practices and both can be valid
PW.2.c Consumes a variety of healthy foods and develops positive eating behaviors. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Names foods from all major food groups and categorizes them
  • Describes why some foods support the body's energy and growth
  • Serves own food portions and uses utensils independently
  • Communicates hunger and fullness with vocabulary and self-advocates
Educator Strategies
  • 1Involve children in food preparation whenever possible; hands-on experience builds food knowledge and positive attitudes
  • 2Model trying new foods without pressure by saying something like: 'I'm going to try a tiny taste. Want to try with me?'
  • 3The Division of Responsibility (Satter, 2000): adults decide what food is offered, children decide whether and how much to eat
  • 4Avoid using food as a reward or punishment; doing so undermines healthy eating relationships over time
PW.2.e Participates in regular physical activity with enjoyment and awareness of its benefits. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Participates willingly in structured and unstructured physical activity daily
  • Describes physical activity as enjoyable and beneficial
  • Demonstrates sustained engagement in active play for 20+ continuous minutes
  • Identifies physical activities they enjoy and seeks them out voluntarily
Educator Strategies
  • 1Forty-five minutes of outdoor active play daily is a developmental and health standard, not a reward or a break from learning
  • 2Strong evidence (Hills et al., 2015) links daily physical activity to improved attention, executive function, and academic performance
  • 3Vary the types of physical activity: free play, structured games, movement exploration, and obstacle challenges
  • 4Never use loss of physical activity as a consequence; doing so teaches children that their body's needs are negotiable
PW.2.f Demonstrates understanding of safety practices and applies them in daily life. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • States and follows classroom and outdoor safety rules without adult reminders
  • Understands pedestrian and traffic safety basics
  • Identifies trusted adults to go to for help in different settings
  • Participates in and can explain safety drill procedures
Educator Strategies
  • 1Teach safety rules as community agreements, not adult-imposed restrictions; this builds intrinsic motivation to follow them
  • 2Practice drills calmly and repeatedly so the procedure becomes second nature before any real emergency arises
  • 3Body safety curriculum should include body autonomy and consent, which are among the most evidence-based protective factor approaches available
  • 4Finkelhor (2009): body safety education in early childhood is one of the most evidence-based child protection interventions available
SC

Science

Preschool Track · Ages 3 to 5 · 2 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Science in early childhood is not about memorizing facts but about developing the habits of scientific thinking: curiosity, observation, questioning, prediction, investigation, and evidence-based reasoning. These habits are both intrinsically valuable and deeply connected to academic success across content areas. The National Research Council's Framework for K-12 Science Education (2012) grounds science education in three dimensions: disciplinary core ideas, science and engineering practices, and crosscutting concepts. In early childhood, the science and engineering practices, such as asking questions, planning investigations, analyzing data, and communicating findings, are the primary focus. Piaget's concept of assimilation and accommodation maps directly onto science inquiry: children encounter phenomena that do not match their existing understanding, and must reorganize their thinking. Reggio Emilia's pedagogical approach treats young children as natural scientists and researchers, a principle that is fully consistent with contemporary neuroscience of learning. Trauma-informed science education creates safety to be wrong: the culture of a science-learning classroom must communicate that incorrect hypotheses are not failures but contributions to the investigation.

SoR
Science of Reading connection: Science builds the content knowledge and vocabulary that research identifies as central to reading comprehension. Background knowledge about the world, including scientific concepts, is a primary resource readers draw on to construct meaning from text (Hirsch, 2006).
SC.1.a Explores and investigates objects, materials, and events in the environment. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Uses multiple senses purposefully and systematically to gather information
  • Uses tools to extend observation (magnifying glass, ruler, balance scale)
  • Makes detailed verbal observations and records them through drawing or dictation
  • Compares two or more objects or phenomena using precise descriptive vocabulary
Educator Strategies
  • 1Maintain a science investigation area with rotating materials, tools, and provocation questions
  • 2The teacher's primary role in science is to question rather than explain; 'What do you notice? What do you wonder?' are more powerful than 'Let me show you why'
  • 3Science notebooks build observation literacy even at the preschool level; drawing what you see is a legitimate scientific practice
  • 4NGSS Engineering Practices at preschool: begin with observing and questioning; the entire inquiry cycle will develop through elementary school
SC.1.b Develops ability to reason about cause and effect through observation and investigation. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • States a hypothesis and tests it ('I think it will happen because...')
  • Identifies the cause of an observed phenomenon using evidence
  • Designs a simple test to answer a question with adult support
  • Distinguishes between variables in a simple investigation with guidance
Educator Strategies
  • 1Causal language is scientific language; teach 'because,' 'causes,' 'makes,' 'so that' as scientific vocabulary
  • 2Use Socratic questioning around cause and effect: 'What do you think made that happen? How could we find out?'
  • 3Use the engineering design cycle for simple projects: identify the problem, brainstorm solutions, test, then reflect and revise
  • 4NRC (2012) identifies cause and effect as one of seven crosscutting concepts connecting all science disciplines; build it in preschool
SE

Social-Emotional Development

Preschool Track · Ages 3 to 5 · 7 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Social-emotional development is not a 'soft' curriculum component; it is the foundation upon which all other learning rests. Harvard's Center on the Developing Child (2020) identifies the serve-and-return interactions between caregivers and children as the primary builder of the neural architecture that enables learning, attention, and regulation. The CASEL (Collaborative for Academic, Social, and Emotional Learning) framework organizes social-emotional competencies into five domains: self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, and responsible decision-making, all of which are addressed in re:Bloom's SE domain. Trauma-informed practice is most critical here: children who have experienced adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) may have fundamentally different stress-response systems that require not punishment and consequence but co-regulation, predictability, and relational safety (Shonkoff & Garner, 2012). Inclusion considerations: social-emotional development cannot be disaggregated from belonging. Children who feel excluded by ability, language, race, gender, or family structure cannot develop the full social-emotional competencies that only safe community makes possible.

SoR
Science of Reading connection: Self-regulation (SE.2.a) and executive function are deeply interconnected with early literacy learning. Children who can regulate attention, manage frustration during challenging tasks, and persist through difficulty learn to read faster and more successfully (Blair & Razza, 2007).
SE.1.a Develops and expands understanding of oneself as a unique and capable person. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Describes own characteristics, preferences, abilities, and family with specificity
  • Understands that personal characteristics make each person unique and valuable
  • Identifies personal strengths and names areas still developing with equanimity
  • Connects personal identity to family culture, language, and community background
Educator Strategies
  • 1Identity-affirming curriculum is not a special topic; it is the baseline of respectful early childhood practice
  • 2Anti-Bias Education (Derman-Sparks & Edwards, 2010) Framework: children develop identities and need mirror texts, images, and conversations that affirm who they are
  • 3Avoid colorblindness; research shows children notice race, gender, and ability at very young ages; pretending otherwise leaves them without language for what they observe
  • 4'All About Me' work is most meaningful when it celebrates specificity: this family, this name's meaning, this tradition, this language
SE.1.b Develops understanding of emotions in self and others. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Names and identifies a range of emotions beyond basic happy/sad/mad/scared
  • Identifies the cause of an emotional experience ('I'm frustrated because...')
  • Recognizes emotions in others from facial cues, body language, and verbal expression
  • Uses feeling vocabulary in daily interactions across contexts
Educator Strategies
  • 1Teach an emotion vocabulary of at least 20 words before kindergarten; research links emotion vocabulary to social competence and academic success
  • 2Use books, puppets, role play, and real moments as equally valid teaching opportunities for emotional literacy
  • 3RULER approach (Brackett, 2019): Recognize, Understand, Label, Express, and Regulate: a full cycle of emotional competence
  • 4Zones of Regulation and similar frameworks are useful structures but should never become punitive (a child in 'the red zone' is not misbehaving; they are struggling)
SE.2.a Develops the capacity to manage emotions and regulate behavior. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Uses 2 or more self-regulation strategies independently when distressed
  • Waits for a turn without adult prompting in most contexts
  • Recovers from emotional upset within a reasonable time frame without adult escalation
  • Identifies own regulation strategies and names them: 'When I'm mad I take breaths'
Educator Strategies
  • 1Regulation must be taught and practiced when children are calm, not only in the moment of dysregulation
  • 2Co-regulation comes before self-regulation; children borrow the calm of a regulated adult before they have developed their own capacity
  • 3Avoid time-out and similar exclusionary practices; the research (Smith et al., 2004) shows they increase dysregulation and decrease trust
  • 4Sensorimotor strategies are particularly effective: deep breathing, movement, weighted items, quiet spaces; the body regulates the brain
SE.3.a Develops empathy, perspective-taking, and understanding of others. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Consistently shows concern for others' feelings without adult prompting
  • Identifies how others might feel in a given situation using evidence
  • Offers comfort or assistance to a peer who is distressed
  • Understands that others can feel differently about the same situation
Educator Strategies
  • 1Read aloud books with complex emotional situations and discuss characters' feelings from multiple perspectives
  • 2Perspective-taking is a cognitive skill that develops gradually through preschool; it cannot be demanded, only nurtured
  • 3Vivian Paley's Rule ('You can't say you can't play') is both an empathy curriculum and an inclusion practice
  • 4Research (Eisenberg et al., 2015): children with higher empathy have significantly better academic outcomes, peer relationships, and mental health across development
SE.4.a Develops secure, trusting relationships with adults in the program. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Maintains a positive and secure relationship with the primary teacher
  • Seeks adult support for academic and social challenges without hesitation
  • Responds positively to teacher warmth and encouragement
  • Uses the adult relationship as a stable base for exploration and risk-taking
Educator Strategies
  • 1Teacher-child relationship quality is the single most powerful variable in the early childhood classroom (Pianta et al., 2008)
  • 2Invest in brief, intentional 2-minute connections with every child daily through greeting, shared reading, or conversation
  • 3Relationship repair after conflict matters more than conflict prevention; model how to repair relationships openly
  • 4Attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth) applied to classroom: a secure teacher-child relationship creates a secure base for all learning
SE.4.b Develops socially competent behaviors with peers. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Initiates and sustains cooperative play with peers
  • Compromises and negotiates in peer play without adult mediation most of the time
  • Shows preference for specific friends and maintains reciprocal friendships
  • Invites others into play and responds warmly to peer invitations
Educator Strategies
  • 1Peer relationships are curriculum; protect and facilitate them rather than over-directing them
  • 2Teach friendship skills explicitly: how to invite, how to join, how to compromise, how to exit gracefully
  • 3Mixed-age groupings (when possible) support social learning: older children develop leadership and younger children receive mentorship
  • 4CASEL research: social competence interventions in preschool produce academic benefits 18 months later; this is not separate from academic work
SE.4.c Develops the ability to navigate and resolve conflicts constructively. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Attempts verbal negotiation before seeking adult help
  • Uses 2 or more conflict resolution strategies independently
  • Accepts a peer's solution and moves forward without lingering resentment
  • Reflects on a conflict and identifies what they might do differently next time
Educator Strategies
  • 1Teach a specific conflict resolution process and use it consistently: stop, tell what happened, listen, and solve it together
  • 2The goal is conflict competence rather than conflict avoidance; children who are never allowed to face conflict never develop the skills to navigate it
  • 3Peace corners and problem-solving spaces give children a place to go when heated; the environment is part of the curriculum
  • 4Research on social problem-solving (Shure, 2001): children who learn problem-solving steps in preschool have lower rates of aggression and higher rates of prosocial behavior through adolescence
SS

Social Studies

Preschool Track · Ages 3 to 5 · 4 standards

Domain Philosophy and Research Base

Social Studies in early childhood lays the foundation for civic identity, historical thinking, geographic awareness, and economic reasoning. The National Council for the Social Studies (2019) explicitly advocates for social studies in early childhood as essential, not supplemental. Young children are natural social scientists: they are intensely curious about people, relationships, communities, rules, fairness, and the physical world they inhabit. Howard Gardner's interpersonal and intrapersonal intelligences are most directly exercised in social studies content. Anti-bias education principles (Derman-Sparks & Edwards, 2010) are foundational to this domain: children are not color-blind, and social studies that ignores systemic inequality, cultural diversity, or the reality of children's lived experiences fails them as learners and citizens. Trauma-informed social studies recognizes that discussions of community, family, helpers, and rules may surface painful realities for some children; approach these topics with awareness, honesty, and care.

SoR
Science of Reading connection: Social Studies builds the background knowledge and world knowledge that research identifies as central to reading comprehension. Knowledge of community, culture, history, and place gives readers the schemas they need to construct meaning from text about the social world.
SS.1.a Develops awareness of own culture and characteristics of diverse groups of people. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Describes own family, culture, traditions, and community with specificity
  • Shows genuine curiosity about and respect for others' cultures, languages, and family structures
  • Makes connections between own and others' families, identifying similarities and differences
  • Uses culturally respectful language when talking about people who are different from themselves
Educator Strategies
  • 1Cultural diversity is not a February or March topic; it is woven into the daily fabric of an anti-bias classroom
  • 2Collect books, materials, and images that authentically represent the full diversity of the world rather than offering a curated or tokenistic version
  • 3Derman-Sparks & Edwards (2010): children need windows (books about others) and mirrors (books about themselves) in equal measure
  • 4Address cultural stereotyping directly when it appears in materials or conversation; silence communicates approval
SS.1.b Develops a basic understanding of needs, wants, and economic concepts. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Identifies basic needs (food, water, shelter, love, safety) and distinguishes them from wants
  • Understands that people make choices because resources are limited
  • Connects community helper roles to meeting people's needs
  • Understands basic concepts of exchange: trading, buying, and giving
Educator Strategies
  • 1Dramatic play is the primary vehicle for economics learning: the classroom restaurant, store, and post office are economic laboratories
  • 2Teach needs vs. wants through authentic moments: 'Do we need a fourth box of crayons, or do we want it?'
  • 3Community helper studies should go beyond traditional roles to include non-traditional occupations and community-based workers
  • 4Economics for early childhood (NCSS, 2019): scarcity, choice, and exchange are the three foundational concepts
SS.1.c Develops understanding that all people have rights and responsibilities within groups. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • States and follows classroom rules without adult prompting most of the time
  • Takes ownership of classroom responsibilities and jobs
  • Explains why rules and responsibilities matter for the wellbeing of the group
  • Participates meaningfully in creating classroom agreements
Educator Strategies
  • 1Classroom rules co-created with children are more internalized than rules handed down; invest the time
  • 2Connect classroom community to civic community; for example: 'We care for our classroom the same way neighbors care for a shared park'
  • 3Introduce the language of rights alongside responsibilities; preschoolers can understand that everyone in the group deserves respect and safety
  • 4NCSS (2019): civic participation begins with the preschool classroom community; treat it as the laboratory for democratic life
SS.1.d Develops ability to take care of materials, the classroom, and the shared environment. ⌄
Developmental Indicators
  • Returns materials to proper locations independently after use
  • Handles books, tools, and materials with demonstrated care
  • Takes initiative to repair or report damaged materials rather than abandoning them
  • Participates in caring for living things in the classroom environment
Educator Strategies
  • 1Montessori's foundational insight: materials that are treated with respect invite respectful use; invest in real, beautiful, complete materials
  • 2Teach care of materials explicitly and repeatedly; it is not a character trait but a learned skill
  • 3Environmental stewardship begins in the classroom; when children care for their room, the garden, and a class pet they build an orientation toward the larger world
  • 4Connect care of the classroom to care of the community and planet; environmental education, social studies, and citizenship are inseparable at this age
