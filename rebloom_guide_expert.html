Introduction: How This Guide Is Organized

This guide is written for educators with formal training in early childhood education or child development, or for experienced practitioners who have already worked through the re:Bloom Novice Implementation Guide. It assumes familiarity with developmental theory, curriculum planning, assessment documentation, and the science of reading. This guide does not repeat what the Novice Guide covers; it deepens it.

Each section connects re:Bloom's curriculum structure to the research base, extends implementation into more complex professional territory, and provides the analytical tools experienced educators need to use this curriculum with the full depth of their training. The annotated sample days in Sections 4 and 5 present educator-child interactions side by side with their theoretical and research rationale, making the implicit explicit.

This guide covers both the Preschool track (Ages 3-5) and the Infant and Toddler track (Birth-36 Months). Each section is organized to address both tracks, with clear labels where content differs. A supplemental section on Family Child Care advanced practice follows Section 5 and addresses the unique professional demands of the mixed-age, home-based setting.

Re:Bloom's nine developmental domains are not arbitrary organizational categories. They reflect a synthesis of the most influential developmental frameworks in the field and their corresponding research bases. Understanding the theoretical origin of each domain strengthens implementation because it clarifies why the domain exists, what it predicts, and how it connects to other domains.

1.2 Standard Code Structure

Re:Bloom standard codes follow a consistent three-part structure: Domain Code, Standard Number, Indicator Letter. For example, LL.2.b means Language and Literacy domain, Standard 2 (phonological awareness), indicator b (onset and rime awareness). This structure enables precise documentation, targeted planning, and cross-referencing with state early learning standards.

When documenting child progress for QRIS, IEP, IFSP, or portfolio purposes, always cite the full standard code alongside the observation. This creates an auditable trail between observation and curriculum alignment that quality assessors and licensing reviewers expect to see.

Research
The use of standardized developmental codes in documentation is associated with higher inter-rater reliability in assessment, stronger family communication, and more targeted professional development (Pianta et al., 2008).
1.3 Play-Based Learning: Theoretical and Empirical Basis

Re:Bloom is explicitly play-based, and this choice is grounded in the deepest and most replicated findings in developmental science rather than in ideology. The case for play-based learning rests on four distinct research pillars:

  • Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development: complex, scaffolded play creates the optimal learning conditions by operating just beyond the child's current independent capacity while providing the social and material support needed to succeed.
  • Piaget's constructivism: children build knowledge through active manipulation of materials and ideas. Direct instruction without concrete experience produces surface-level recall, not understanding.
  • Executive function research (Diamond, 2013; Center on the Developing Child, 2020): pretend play specifically develops the cognitive flexibility, working memory, and inhibitory control that predict academic and life outcomes.
  • Longitudinal outcome data (Marcon, 2002; Stipek et al., 1995): children from play-based preschool programs show equal or superior academic outcomes compared to academically-oriented programs by third grade, with significantly better social-emotional outcomes and motivation.

This does not mean play is undirected. Re:Bloom distinguishes among three types of play, all of which are part of a high-quality implementation:

Play TypeDefinitionEducator RoleExample
Child-directed free playChild initiates, directs, and sustains; adult does not intervene unless safety requires itObserve, document, prepare the environment, add materials that extendChild builds elaborate block city over multiple days
Facilitated playAdult enters child's play to extend, deepen, or introduce new vocabulary and concepts without taking overAsk open-ended questions, introduce vocabulary, add a prop or challengeAdult joins the block city builder: 'What do people in this city need? Where will they get food?'
Guided playAdult sets up an experience with a learning objective; child explores freely within that structureSet up the provocation, observe, document, respond to what children do with itWater table with funnels, tubes, and measuring cups set up to explore capacity

2.2 Inquiry-Based Monthly Themes: Design Principles

Re:Bloom's monthly planning is organized around inquiry themes rather than topic-based units. The distinction is significant and has research implications. A topic-based unit (e.g., Apples in October) presents children with predetermined information. An inquiry-based theme (e.g., Growing Things) poses a generative question and allows children to investigate it across multiple domains over time.

A well-designed re:Bloom inquiry theme has five characteristics:

  • Generative: it opens into genuine questions that children find worth asking
  • Cross-domain: it connects authentically to all nine domains, not just science or social studies
  • Child-accessible: children can investigate it through direct experience, not just books or pictures
  • Sustained: it can deepen over four weeks rather than being exhausted in one
  • Culturally responsive: it connects to children's actual lives, families, and communities

2.3 Differentiating Within the Developmental Continuum

The indicators in each standard are organized developmentally, not by age. This means the same standard applies to children across a developmental range, with different children working on different indicators within that standard. This is the primary mechanism for differentiation in re:Bloom.

For educators working with mixed-ability or mixed-age groups, the planning task is to identify where each child is within the continuum and plan experiences that offer multiple entry points. Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development is the operational principle: the same material can be experienced at different levels of complexity by different children simultaneously.

Reflect
Reflective question: In your current classroom, identify one standard where children in your group are at three or more different indicator levels. How does your environment currently support all three levels? What is missing?

Section 3: Science of Reading Implementation Across Both Tracks

3.1 The SoR Architecture in Re:Bloom

Scarborough's Reading Rope (2001) provides the conceptual architecture for re:Bloom's SoR integration. The Rope identifies two strands that must be woven together for skilled reading: word recognition (phonological awareness, decoding, sight recognition) and language comprehension (background knowledge, vocabulary, verbal reasoning, literacy knowledge). Both strands are developed from birth through the full re:Bloom curriculum, not only in the LL domain.

The Simple View of Reading (Gough and Tunmer, 1986) formalizes this as: Reading Comprehension = Decoding x Language Comprehension. This multiplicative relationship means that weakness in either factor limits reading comprehension regardless of strength in the other. A child who decodes fluently but has limited vocabulary and background knowledge will plateau in comprehension. A child with rich language and background knowledge who cannot decode is equally limited. Re:Bloom builds both strands simultaneously from birth.

3.2 Developmental SoR Sequence: Infant and Toddler Track

The SoR foundation for the birth-to-36-month period is entirely oral and auditory. No child under three should be receiving explicit phonics instruction. The appropriate SoR work at this age is:

Age BandPrimary SoR FocusRe:Bloom StandardsKey Practices
Birth to 6 monthsProsodic sensitivity: responding to rhythm, melody, and stress patterns in speechLL.1.a, LL.1.cInfant-directed speech (motherese); lullabies; varied vocal register; serve-and-return
6 to 12 monthsPhonological discrimination: distinguishing phonemes in the ambient languageLL.1.a, LL.1.b, LL.2.aDaily song; varied phonological input; naming routines; joint attention with books
12 to 24 monthsLexical development and early phonological awareness through rhyme and songLL.1.b, LL.1.c, LL.2.cNursery rhymes; repetitive books; vocabulary narration; dialogic reading with board books
24 to 36 monthsSyllable awareness; onset recognition; rhyme detection and productionLL.2.a, LL.2.b, LL.2.cSyllable clapping; alliterative language; rhyming books with prediction; phonological games
Research
Kilpatrick (2015): phonological sensitivity begins in infancy through exposure to the prosodic and phonological properties of spoken language. The investment in rich, varied language input in the first three years is the most cost-effective literacy intervention available.
3.3 Developmental SoR Sequence: Preschool Track

At the preschool level, phonological awareness instruction becomes explicit and intentional while remaining playful. The sequence follows the well-established hierarchy from large to small phonological units:

Sequence LevelPhonological UnitRe:Bloom StandardInstructional Focus
1 - FoundationalWord and syllableLL.2.aIdentifying words in sentences; clapping syllables; counting syllables in names and familiar words
2 - Onset and RimeOnset + rimeLL.2.bBlending onset and rime (/h/ + /at/ = hat); identifying common rimes (-at, -an, -ot); generating words with a target rime
3 - RhymeRhyming pairsLL.2.cRecognizing rhyming pairs; generating rhymes; oddity tasks (which one does not rhyme?)
4 - Initial PhonemeFirst soundLL.2.bIsolating initial consonant sounds; sorting by initial sound; generating words that begin with a target sound
5 - BlendingPhoneme to wordLL.3.b (bridge)Blending two to three phonemes into a word; connecting to letter-sound knowledge once stable

This sequence is not rigid: some children arrive at preschool with strong phonological awareness and move quickly; others need more time at the syllable and onset-rime level. The daily 10-minute phonological awareness block should be differentiated across small groups. The whole-group phonological awareness activity (during morning meeting or transitions) should target the majority of the group's current level.

3.4 Vocabulary Instruction: The Tier Framework

Beck, McKeown, and Kucan's (2013) three-tier vocabulary framework provides the instructional architecture for re:Bloom vocabulary work across both tracks:

  • Tier 1: High-frequency everyday words (dog, run, big, happy). These are acquired naturally through exposure and rarely require explicit instruction. For dual language learners, however, Tier 1 words in the second language require intentional instruction.
  • Tier 2: Cross-context academic words (investigate, compare, construct, estimate, predict, categorize). These appear across subject areas and are highly predictive of academic reading comprehension. They require explicit, repeated instruction. Re:Bloom's vocabulary focus is primarily Tier 2.
  • Tier 3: Domain-specific technical vocabulary (photosynthesis, cardinality, alliteration). These emerge naturally from inquiry themes and should be taught in context; they are not the primary instructional focus at this age.

Effective Tier 2 vocabulary instruction at the preschool level has six components (Beck et al., 2013; Biemiller and Slonim, 2001):

  • Introduce the word with a child-accessible definition
  • Use it in a sentence that clarifies meaning through context
  • Ask children to use it in their own sentence
  • Connect it to a physical gesture, image, or concrete referent
  • Revisit it in multiple contexts across the week
  • Create opportunities for children to hear and use it naturally during play and routine

Section 4: Annotated Sample Day - Preschool

This section presents a complete preschool day using the Living Things inquiry theme. Each key interaction is presented in two columns: what the educator says or does on the left, and the theoretical and research rationale on the right. The goal is to make the implicit expertise of skilled early childhood teaching visible and transferable.

7:30 AM - Arrival and Morning Greeting
Educator greets each child at the door by name, gets to child level, and makes individual eye contact before allowing entry.
Educator Says
Educator: 'Good morning, Marcus. I was thinking about you this morning. I saved a special magnifying glass for you at the discovery table. Go find it.'
Why It Matters
Pianta et al. (2008): individualized, warm greeting is among the highest-leverage CLASS Positive Climate and Teacher Sensitivity behaviors. The specific, personal message communicates that the educator knows the child as an individual, which is the foundation of the secure base from which learning proceeds.
Child arrives reluctant. Educator does not push past the emotion.
Educator Says
Educator: 'You are not ready yet. I can see that. I am going to stay right here with you until you are ready. There is no rush.'
Why It Matters
Attachment theory (Bowlby, 1969; Ainsworth et al., 1978): a secure base response meets the child's attachment need before the learning need. Attempting to redirect before the child is regulated is neurobiologically counterproductive; the stress-response system must return to baseline before the prefrontal cortex can support learning.
8:00 AM - Morning Meeting
Educator reads the morning message aloud, pointing to each word. Text: 'Good morning, scientists. What is the difference between living and non-living?'
Educator Says
Educator: 'I am going to point to each word as I read. Watch my finger. Good. Morning. Scientists. That word is scientists. What do scientists do?'
Why It Matters
Clay's Concepts About Print (2000): directionality tracking and print-to-speech matching during shared reading builds print concept knowledge without explicit instruction. The morning message is a daily opportunity for this. LL.3.a indicators are addressed through consistent, brief, non-evaluative print tracking.
Educator introduces vocabulary. Target words: organism, habitat, survive.
Educator Says
Educator: 'I am going to teach you a science word. The word is organism. Say it: organism. An organism is any living thing. You are an organism. The tree outside is an organism. The worm in our science area is an organism. Turn to your partner and tell them: are you an organism?'
Why It Matters
Beck et al. (2013) Tier 2 instruction: child-accessible definition, immediate use in sentences, partner practice. The physical affirmation ('you are an organism') creates an embodied memory anchor. Research shows vocabulary learned through embodied and social activity is retained significantly longer than vocabulary learned through definition alone (Goldin-Meadow, 2014).
8:20 AM - Intentional Read-Aloud
Before reading: educator activates background knowledge and previews vocabulary.
Educator Says
Educator: 'Before I open the book, look at the cover for ten seconds. Do not tell me anything yet. Just look. [Pause.] Now: what did you notice? What do you think this book is going to be about? What makes you think that?'
Why It Matters
Interactive Read-Aloud research (Beck and McKeown, 2001): activating prior knowledge before reading prepares the language comprehension strand by creating schema into which new information can be organized. The 10-second silent observation before discussion builds observational patience, a precursor to scientific practice.
During reading: educator stops at key vocabulary and uses PEER sequence.
Educator Says
Educator: [stops at the word 'camouflage'] 'This word is camouflage. Can you say that? What do you think it means based on this picture?' [Child: 'It is hiding?'] 'Exactly right. Camouflage is when an animal uses color or shape to blend in with its surroundings so predators cannot see it. Look at this frog. Can you see where its body is against the leaf?'
Why It Matters
PEER (Prompt, Evaluate, Expand, Repeat) from Dialogic Reading research (Whitehurst et al., 1994): prompting child response before providing the definition activates retrieval practice and positions the child as a meaning-maker rather than a passive recipient. The expansion extends the child's partial understanding into a full, technically accurate definition.
After reading: educator extends meaning through text-to-world connection.
Educator Says
Educator: 'The animals in this book all have bodies that help them survive in their habitat. Think about your body. What parts of your body help you survive? Turn and talk: one body part and what it helps you do.'
Why It Matters
Background knowledge building (Hirsch, 2006; Nation and Snowling, 2004): connecting text to personal experience builds the world knowledge that reading comprehension depends on. The text-to-self connection is the most accessible connection for preschoolers, and it activates the SE and PW domains simultaneously with LL.
8:40 AM - Phonological Awareness Block (10 minutes)
Whole-group activity: onset isolation with living things vocabulary.
Educator Says
Educator: 'Scientist word game. I am going to say a word. You tell me the first sound. LEAF. [Pause for children to respond.] /L/. Right. LIZARD. /L/. LADYBUG. /L/. Now I am going to say a word that starts with something different. Ready? SNAKE. [Pause.] /S/! Good listening. What sound did snake start with?'
Why It Matters
Kilpatrick (2015) phonological awareness hierarchy: initial phoneme isolation at this level targets the onset of words. This is the appropriate sequence for late preschool, following established syllable and rhyme awareness. Embedding phonological awareness in content vocabulary (science words) simultaneously develops phonological skill and vocabulary depth.
Segmenting practice for children who are ready for phoneme-level work.
Educator Says
Educator: [to small group during transitions] 'Let us do a sound-stretching game. I am going to stretch a word like a rubber band. Watch: /l/-/i/-/v/. What word is that? LIVE! Now you try: stretch the word BUG. How many sounds did you hear?'
Why It Matters
Elkonin boxes research (Clay, 2000; Kilpatrick, 2015): phoneme segmentation is the most predictive phonological awareness skill for early decoding. Differentiated practice during transitions provides phoneme-level work for children who have mastered onset-rime without pulling the whole group to a level that is above most children's current zone.
9:00 AM - Center Time (60-90 minutes uninterrupted)
Educator circulates rather than managing. Observes before entering.
Educator Says
Educator observes two children at the science area without intervening for three minutes. Writes anecdotal note: 'Amara and Deja sorting organism picture cards, created three categories: 'moves,' 'grows,' 'both.' Neither category was suggested by the adult. Amara: CO.3.a - Developing; MA.3.b - Consistently Observed.'
Why It Matters
Reggio Emilia observation practice: sustained observation before intervention reveals children's actual competence levels and emergent theories. The documented observation serves three purposes simultaneously: it is assessment data (CO.3.a, MA.3.b), curriculum information (children can sort by self-generated categories), and evidence for QRIS documentation.
Educator enters Amara and Deja's play to extend, not redirect.
Educator Says
Educator: 'I have been watching you. You made a category that says both moves AND grows. Can you tell me what goes in that one?' [Children explain.] 'So you are saying something can do both at the same time. That is really sophisticated scientific thinking. A scientist would call those organisms that both move and grow... well, actually, what would YOU call them?'
Why It Matters
Vygotsky's ZPD (1978): the educator's question 'what would YOU call them?' extends the children's thinking rather than providing the answer. This is facilitated play in which the educator operates within the child-initiated frame, adding complexity without taking over. CLASS Instructional Support: Concept Development behaviors (CO.3.a, SC.1.a).
CLASS
CLASS Instructional Support - Concept Development: this interaction demonstrates Analysis and Reasoning (children are categorizing), Connections (linking organisms to their properties), and Creation (children invented novel categories). This is the highest-scoring Concept Development behavior in the CLASS tool.
10:30 AM - Small Group Instruction
Vocabulary-focused small group (4-6 children at similar language levels).
Educator Says
Educator: 'I have four objects here. I am going to use all three of our vocabulary words in one sentence. See if you can do the same. Watch me first: The worm is an organism that lives in a soil habitat and survives by eating decomposed leaves. Now you try. Take one object and make a sentence with at least two of our words.'
Why It Matters
Beck et al. (2013): producing vocabulary in syntactically complex sentences is the highest level of vocabulary instruction and demonstrates deep word knowledge. Modeling the target performance first (I Do) before asking children to attempt it (You Do) follows explicit instruction research on gradual release of responsibility.
11:15 AM - Outdoor Time (45 minutes minimum)
Educator sets up an investigation protocol before going outside.
Educator Says
Educator: 'Before we go outside, scientists need a question. Our question today is: how many different organisms can we find in one square foot of ground? I am going to give each of you a hula hoop. Place it on the ground. That is your investigation square. Everything inside that hoop is your study area.'
Why It Matters
NGSS science practices (NRC, 2012): this structured outdoor investigation addresses Asking Questions (framing the inquiry), Planning and Carrying Out Investigations (systematic observation within a defined area), and Analyzing and Interpreting Data (counting and categorizing). The hula hoop boundary makes the investigation manageable and introduces a fundamental science concept: the study plot.
During investigation, educator uses Socratic questioning rather than information-giving.
Educator Says
Educator: [child finds a beetle] 'Interesting. Before you tell me what you think that is, describe it to me. What do you notice? How many body parts? How many legs? What does it do when you move close to it? What do you think all of those things are telling you about where it lives?'
Why It Matters
Science inquiry pedagogy (Chin and Osborne, 2008): sequencing observation before identification trains the scientific habit of evidence-before-conclusion. The educator's refusal to name the organism is intentional; providing the name before the child has fully observed short-circuits the inquiry process and positions the adult as the authority rather than the process.
Afternoon: Mathematics Focus, Closing Meeting, Departure
Mathematics: using investigation data to build number sense and data literacy.
Educator Says
Educator: 'Let us look at everyone's data. How many organisms did each person find in their hoop? Let us write the numbers on this chart. [Creates a simple data display.] Now look at the chart. What do you notice? What do you wonder? Which hoop had the most organisms? How many more did [Name] find than [Name]?'
Why It Matters
Clements and Sarama (2014) learning trajectories: data representation and comparison are components of the MA.2.a and MA.3.b standards and emerge naturally from authentic investigation. Posing 'what do you notice, what do you wonder' frames mathematical conversation as inquiry, which is both more engaging and more aligned with contemporary mathematics education research than rote computation practice.
Closing meeting: plan-do-review and forward bridge.
Educator Says
Educator: 'This morning you told me your theories about what makes something alive. Look at this chart. You said: it moves, it eats, it breathes, it grows. Now that we have investigated all day, are there any theories you want to change? Any evidence we found that changes your thinking?'
Why It Matters
HighScope plan-do-review (Schweinhart et al., 2005): the recall and review cycle builds metacognitive awareness of one's own learning process, which research identifies as a significant predictor of academic self-regulation. Returning to children's initial theories and revisiting them with evidence is authentic scientific reasoning and creates intellectual closure for the day.

Section 5: Annotated Sample Day - Infant and Toddler

The infant and toddler annotated day focuses on caregiving interactions because, at this age, caregiving IS curriculum. Each annotation connects a specific caregiving behavior to its developmental and neuroscientific rationale. The theme used throughout is Textures and Touch, appropriate for both age bands.

INFANT SECTION: Annotated Caregiving Interactions
Arrival: primary caregiver receives infant from family.
Educator Says
Caregiver: 'Good morning, Lily. I see you. [Picks up infant, makes eye contact, waits for eye contact to be established.] There you are. Let us say goodbye to Daddy. [Holds infant facing father, allows goodbye, then turns infant to face the room.] Here we go. I wonder what we are going to explore today.'
Why It Matters
Bowlby (1969), Ainsworth et al. (1978): the arrival transition is the most neurobiologically significant moment of the infant's day. The caregiver's calm, warm, immediate response establishes or maintains the secure attachment bond that is the prerequisite for all exploration and learning. Rushing this transition for efficiency is not a neutral choice; it is a developmental one.
Floor time: caregiver sets up texture basket and observes before interacting.
Educator Says
Caregiver places basket with soft fabric, smooth stone, rough burlap, and a small bristle brush within infant's reach. Sits nearby without initiating interaction. Notes: 'Lily reached for smooth stone first. Held it in both hands. Transferred to left hand. Brought it to mouth. Reached for burlap. Withdrew hand quickly. Made a face.'
Why It Matters
Ayres Sensory Integration (1979); Montessori (1949): the intentional materials selection and non-interventive observation allows the caregiver to assess the infant's current sensory processing preferences and tolerances. The infant's withdrawal from the rough burlap is data, not a problem to fix. This observation documents PW.1.d (sensory guidance of movement) and informs the next materials selection.
Serve-and-return during floor play.
Educator Says
Infant vocalizes while holding the stone. Caregiver: 'Oh! You have something to tell me about that stone. [Pause.] I heard you. Tell me more. [Pause. Infant vocalizes again.] You said [imitates vocalization]. Is it heavy? Is it smooth? [Runs infant's hand over stone.] Smooth. That stone is smooth.'
Why It Matters
Harvard Center on the Developing Child (2021): serve-and-return interaction builds neural architecture for language, cognition, and social-emotional development. The caregiver's imitation of the vocalization before providing the vocabulary word models that the infant's communication has value and elicits further communication. This is the optimal sequence: honor the communication, then extend it. Three to four complete serve-and-return exchanges per minute with a focused caregiver represents an optimal language environment (Suskind, 2015).
Diapering as curriculum: narrated, consensual, relational.
Educator Says
Caregiver: 'Lily, I am going to change your diaper now. I am going to pick you up. Ready? Up. Now I am laying you down. Can you feel the cool of the table? I am taking off your diaper. I see your tummy. [Touch gently.] There is your belly button. Now the clean diaper. Can you feel that? All clean. I am going to wash my hands. Can you see me at the sink? [Returns.] All done. You were so patient.'
Why It Matters
Trauma-informed care (Shonkoff and Garner, 2012): consensual narration of physical caregiving establishes body safety, body knowledge (PW.2.a), and trust in the caregiver. For infants who have experienced early adversity or medical trauma, predictable, warm narration during physical caregiving is the primary relational intervention available. Every caregiving routine is an attachment intervention.
TODDLER SECTION: Annotated Interactions
Morning circle: vocabulary introduction through sensory experience.
Educator Says
Caregiver: [holds up burlap] 'I am going to pass this around. When it gets to you, touch it and tell me one word. Just one word about how it feels.' [Children: rough, scratchy, itchy, hard.] 'All of those words describe texture. Say that word: texture. Texture means how something feels when you touch it. What is the texture of this burlap?'
Why It Matters
Beck et al. (2013): introducing 'texture' as a Tier 2 vocabulary word through direct sensory experience is the most powerful available instructional approach for toddlers. The embodied experience creates a durable sensory memory anchor for the word. Eliciting children's own descriptive language before providing the technical term validates their linguistic competence while extending it.
Conflict mediation during center time.
Educator Says
Two toddlers both want the same smooth stone from the texture basket. Caregiver: [gets to child level] 'I see you both want the stone. Marcus, you got it first. Deja, you want it. Both of you want this stone. [Pause, does not solve immediately.] Marcus, what can you do? Deja, what can you do? I will wait.'
Why It Matters
ICPS - I Can Problem Solve (Shure, 2001): waiting after posing the question, rather than solving it for the children, is the critical instructional move. Research shows that children who are consistently given the solution to conflicts do not develop the problem-solving neural pathways that generate solutions independently. The five-second wait is cognitively uncomfortable for the adult and developmentally essential for the children.
Language extension during play: expanding utterances systematically.
Educator Says
Toddler: 'Soft.' [Holding the velvet fabric.] Caregiver: 'That fabric is so soft. The velvet fabric is very soft. Softer than the burlap. Which one do you like better, the soft velvet or the rough burlap?'
Why It Matters
Vygotsky (1978) ZPD; language expansion research (Fey et al., 2013): expanding a one-word utterance to a five-word sentence by adding grammatical complexity rather than correcting the child's form is the most effective language facilitation strategy available to caregivers. The expansion models the target without marking the child's utterance as wrong, which maintains communicative motivation.
Rest time: co-regulation before self-regulation.
Educator Says
Toddler is resisting rest. Caregiver: 'Your body is tired and you do not want to stop. That is a hard feeling. I am going to sit next to your cot. You do not have to sleep. I am going to stay here with you while you rest.' [Sits beside the toddler, places hand gently on their back, stays silent.]
Why It Matters
Diamond (2013) executive function; Center on the Developing Child (2020) co-regulation: the caregiver's physical presence and regulated nervous system is literally borrowed by the toddler's dysregulated nervous system. This is not indulgence; it is the mechanism by which self-regulation capacity is built. A toddler who is forced to self-regulate before they have the neurological capacity to do so learns helplessness, not regulation.

Section 5b: The Family Child Care Setting — Advanced Practice

FCC
This section addresses the Family Child Care setting specifically. If you work in a center-based program, you may skip this section or read it to understand how re:Bloom supports FCC providers.

The family child care setting presents a unique professional challenge: it demands simultaneous mastery of developmental knowledge across the full birth-to-five span, applied within a single physical space to a mixed-age group, by a single educator operating without colleagues, supervisors, or professional community in the room. Research consistently shows that the highest-quality family child care produces child outcomes equivalent to the best center-based care. The difference is almost entirely a function of the provider's professional knowledge and intentionality. This section addresses what that looks like in practice.

Theoretical Grounding for the Mixed-Age Environment

Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development is operating at every moment in a mixed-age FCC home. When a preschooler explains to a toddler why the ice is melting, both children are working in their ZPD: the preschooler consolidates understanding through teaching, and the toddler receives exposure to thinking that slightly exceeds their independent capacity. Cross-age interaction is not a logistical inconvenience. It is one of the most powerful pedagogical structures available in early childhood education, and it is the natural condition of the family child care home.

Research
Howes and Wishard Guerra (2004): family child care providers with higher professional knowledge and intentionality produce significantly better child outcomes across language, social-emotional, and cognitive domains. The single most important variable in FCC quality is provider professional knowledge, which is precisely what re:Bloom is designed to build and support.
Planning Architecture for Mixed-Age Groups

Expert FCC planning begins not with activities but with the developmental analysis of the group. For each child, what domain is in the zone of proximal development this month? Which skills are emerging and need scaffolding? Which are consolidating and need complexity? The answer to these questions should drive activity selection, not the other way around.

The Science of Reading in a Mixed-Age FCC Home

Implementing a SoR-aligned sequence across three developmental levels simultaneously is the most technically demanding aspect of FCC curriculum. The key is understanding that SoR is a developmental progression, not a single-age curriculum. Infants are building the oral language and serve-and-return interaction that is the foundation of language comprehension. Toddlers are developing phonological sensitivity, vocabulary, and print awareness. Preschoolers are working on the specific phonological awareness skills (syllable, onset-rime, phoneme) that decode instruction will later build on. In a well-functioning FCC home, all three levels are operating simultaneously in every read-aloud and language interaction.

Reflect
Reflective question: In your current FCC group, identify one child at each age level. Trace the SoR developmental continuum for each child: what oral language skills is the infant building? What phonological sensitivity work is most appropriate for the toddler? What phonemic awareness skill is the preschooler ready for? How does your current morning meeting and read-aloud practice address all three levels?
The FCC Provider as Curriculum Instrument

In center-based programs, the curriculum environment carries significant pedagogical weight: the physical classroom, the materials, the centers, the schedule. In a family child care home, the provider is the primary curriculum instrument. The quality of language in every interaction, the warmth and contingency of every response, the questions asked during outdoor time, the narration during caregiving: these are the curriculum. This is both a profound responsibility and an extraordinary professional opportunity. No center-based educator has the depth of relationship and the consistency of presence that a high-quality FCC provider has with the children in their care.

Assessment and Documentation at the Expert Level

Expert-level FCC documentation goes beyond compliance. It is a reflective practice that drives planning. Expert use of this data involves: comparing each child's profile across rating periods to identify growth trajectories, identifying which domains are receiving insufficient curriculum attention across the group, and using the data to justify resource and environment decisions to families and QRIS reviewers.

The most significant structural challenge of family child care is professional isolation. Expert FCC practice requires deliberate strategies to counter this: formal participation in a FCC network or professional association, regular peer consultation with other FCC providers, and engagement with resources like re:Bloom that provide the professional knowledge base that institutional settings supply through supervision and team meetings. Section 7: Environment as Curriculum

7.1 The Prepared Environment: Theoretical Basis

Maria Montessori's prepared environment principle holds that the physical environment is the third teacher (after the primary caregiver and peers). An intentionally prepared environment invites specific behaviors, supports specific developmental goals, and communicates specific values about who children are and what they are capable of. Re:Bloom's environment design is grounded in three principles:

  • Beauty invites care: materials that are beautiful, real, and complete are treated with more respect and engaged with more deeply than plastic or broken materials.
  • Accessibility communicates agency: materials at child height and in open containers communicate that children have the right and responsibility to make choices.
  • Organization communicates expectation: labeled, orderly spaces communicate that children are expected to manage them, and that materials are worth returning to their places.

Section 8: Differentiation, Equity, and Inclusion

8.1 Using the Developmental Continuum for Differentiation

Effective differentiation in re:Bloom does not require separate lesson plans for different children. It requires that every learning experience offers multiple entry points and that the educator's interactions are calibrated to each child's current zone of proximal development. The same block center can support a child working on MA.4.b at the earliest indicator level (exploring spatial relationships by filling and dumping containers) and another child at the most advanced indicator level (creating and following a diagram of a structure). The environment does the differentiation; the educator's observation and responsive interaction sustains it.

8.2 Trauma-Informed Implementation

Children who have experienced adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) present in early childhood settings with a range of behaviors that are frequently misread as defiance, inattention, or willful non-compliance. Understanding the neurobiology of toxic stress (Shonkoff and Garner, 2012) reframes these behaviors as responses to perceived threat rather than as character or motivational problems.

8.3 Anti-Bias and Culturally Responsive Implementation

Derman-Sparks and Edwards (2010) identify four anti-bias goals for early childhood education: positive identity, respect for diversity, critical thinking about bias, and activism. These are not supplemental activities; they are the baseline of developmentally appropriate practice for the full range of children served by licensed child care.

Research documents that children as young as three months show differential responses based on race (Kelly et al., 2005) and that preschool children hold measurable biases that reflect the broader cultural environment they have been raised in (Bigler and Liben, 2007). The educator's role is not to pretend children do not notice these differences but to provide accurate, affirming, and justice-oriented language for what they observe.

Reflect
Reflective question: Walk through your environment with the eyes of every child currently enrolled. Who is represented on the walls? In the books? In the materials? Whose language is heard in the room? Whose family structure is reflected in the dramatic play area? The answers tell you what your room communicates to each child about whether they belong here.
8.4 Supporting Dual Language Learners

Dual language learners (DLLs) are not cognitively delayed; they are cognitively advanced. The executive function demands of managing two linguistic systems simultaneously produce measurable advantages in cognitive flexibility (Bialystok, 2011). However, DLLs may appear to have limited English vocabulary or language development when assessed only in English; this is an assessment artifact, not a developmental fact.

Section 10: Using This Curriculum for Professional Learning

10.1 Reflective Practice Protocols

Reflective practice is not self-evaluation; it is systematic inquiry into one's own teaching. The following protocols are designed to be used individually or with a professional learning community:

Video Analysis Protocol (monthly)

Record 20 minutes of center time or a read-aloud. Watch it twice: first with the sound off to observe your physical positioning, proximity, and non-verbal communication. Watch a second time for language quality: ratio of open-ended to closed questions; frequency of vocabulary extension; evidence of serve-and-return or dialogic reading. Note three strengths and one specific goal.

Child Case Study Protocol (quarterly)

Choose one child whose development puzzles or concerns you. Gather three weeks of anecdotal observations, review their most recent assessment, and write a one-page developmental narrative. Then identify: what is this child's current zone of proximal development in two domains? What environmental change or interactional change would best support their next step? Share with a colleague for feedback.

Domain Deep Dive Protocol (annually, one domain per month)

Choose one domain per month and read one primary research source listed in the Scope and Sequence references. Write a one-paragraph reflection: what in this research changes how I think about this domain? What would I do differently in my environment or interactions?

Closing: On the Craft of Early Childhood Teaching

The research that grounds re:Bloom returns again and again to one conclusion: the quality of the relationship between educator and child is the most powerful variable in early childhood settings. More powerful than the curriculum, more powerful than the environment, more powerful than any individual instructional strategy. This is not a critique of curricula, environments, or strategies; it is a claim about what makes them work.

Pianta and colleagues (2008) found that warm, sensitive, responsive educator-child relationships predict academic and social-emotional outcomes more strongly than any structural or instructional variable measured. Shonkoff and colleagues have documented that the serve-and-return interaction is not just nice to have; it is the biological mechanism through which neural architecture is built. Hart and Risley found that the quality of conversational interaction in the first three years predicts reading achievement at nine years with a correlation that no other variable matches.

Re:Bloom provides the structure, the sequence, the research, the tools, and the language. You bring the relationship. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient alone. The curriculum in your hands is as powerful as the educator who holds it is present, warm, curious, and committed to the children in their care.

That is the work. It is hard, skilled, consequential, and often invisible. This curriculum is designed to make it a little more visible: to name what skilled early childhood educators do, why it matters, and what the research says about its effects. May it serve you well and, through you, the children whose first years you hold.
