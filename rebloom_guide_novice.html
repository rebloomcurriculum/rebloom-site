Section 1: What a Curriculum Is and Why It Matters

What a curriculum is

A curriculum is a plan. It answers three questions:

  • What do children need to learn at this age?
  • How do I help them learn it?
  • How do I know they are making progress?

re:Bloom answers all three questions for children from birth through age five. You do not have to figure this out on your own. The curriculum has already done that work.

What a curriculum is NOT

A curriculum is not a script you follow word-for-word all day. It is not a set of worksheets. It is not a list of things children have to memorize. And it is definitely not something that turns your classroom into a school.

re:Bloom is a play-based curriculum. That means children learn by playing, exploring, building, painting, singing, talking, and moving. Your job is to make sure the play is rich, intentional, and connected to what children actually need to learn at this stage of development.

Remember
Play IS the curriculum. When a three-year-old builds a tower and knocks it down ten times, they are learning physics, persistence, cause and effect, and spatial reasoning. Your job is to notice that and build on it.
The nine areas children learn in

re:Bloom organizes child development into nine areas called domains. Every child, every age, every day, all nine domains. Here they are in plain language:

Domain CodeWhat It Means in Plain Language
AL - Approaches to LearningHow children tackle new things: their curiosity, persistence, and attitude toward learning
CO - Cognitive DevelopmentHow children think: memory, problem-solving, and flexible thinking
CR - Creative DevelopmentHow children express themselves: art, music, dramatic play, and imaginative thinking
LL - Language and LiteracyHow children communicate and build reading foundations: talking, listening, books, sounds, and early writing
MA - MathematicsHow children understand numbers, shapes, patterns, and measurement
PW - Physical Development and WellnessHow children move, take care of their bodies, and stay healthy and safe
SC - ScienceHow children investigate and wonder about the world around them
SE - Social-Emotional DevelopmentHow children understand themselves, manage their feelings, and get along with others
SS - Social StudiesHow children understand their families, communities, and the world of people

Over time you will find yourself naturally covering more standards without even thinking about it. That is how curriculum implementation develops: one intentional choice at a time, until intentionality becomes habit.

Section 3: How to Use re:Bloom Every Day

The five-step weekly rhythm

Here is the complete weekly rhythm for using re:Bloom. Follow these five steps and you will be implementing the curriculum with fidelity.

Step 1: Plan (Sunday evening or Monday morning, 15-20 minutes)

Open your Lesson Plan Template and fill in your plan for the week — or use the AI Lesson Plan Generator in your subscriber portal to generate a complete plan automatically. The generator takes your theme, your children's interests, and any group focus areas and builds a full week with all nine domains covered, SoR alignment, differentiation notes, and a family handout in under 30 seconds. Either way, fill in:

Tip
The AI Lesson Plan Generator is in your subscriber portal under AI-Powered Tools. It generates a complete week — not just activity ideas — including Morning Meeting, Read-Aloud, Phonological Awareness, Center Time, Small Group, and Outdoor blocks, with optional educator scripts and your state's ELDS codes tagged automatically. Most educators find it saves 2 to 3 hours of weekly planning time.
  • Your monthly inquiry theme (found in the Monthly Planning Guide)
  • One or two domain focus areas for the week
  • Three to five activities that connect to your theme
  • What materials you need
  • One family connection idea
Tip
You do not need a different activity for every hour of the day. Three to five good activities across the week is enough. Children learn more from doing the same rich activity multiple times than from doing ten shallow activities once.
Step 2: Set up your room (before children arrive)

Before children arrive, set out materials that connect to your theme and your focus domains. You do not need to change the whole room. Small intentional additions are enough:

  • Put relevant books in the reading corner
  • Add theme-related materials to one or two centers
  • Write a simple morning message on chart paper (preschool)
  • Set out a new sensory material on the discovery table
  • Check that all nine domain areas have something available
Step 3: Be intentional during routines

Every routine of the day is a teaching opportunity. You do not need to stop what you are doing to teach. You teach by narrating, asking questions, and noticing out loud. Here is what this looks like in each routine:

RoutineHow to Be Intentional
ArrivalGreet every child by name. Ask one question about their morning. Name what you notice: 'You look like you have a lot of energy today.'
Morning MeetingConnect to your theme and your focus domain. Ask open-ended questions. Read your morning message together (preschool).
Center Time / Floor PlayMove from area to area. Narrate what you observe. Ask 'What are you making?' rather than 'What is that?' Extend play by adding a prop or asking a question.
Read-AloudStop on every page that matters. Ask 'What do you think will happen?' and 'Why do you think he did that?' Name new vocabulary words before you turn the page.
Meals and SnackTalk during meals. Name foods, textures, colors, amounts. Count crackers. Compare sizes. Math and science are on every plate.
Outdoor TimeGive children time to explore independently. Then join them and ask questions: 'What do you notice about this rock?' 'Why do you think the water is going that way?'
TransitionsUse songs, counting, and rhymes during every transition. These are not fillers; they are phonological awareness instruction.
Caregiving (IT)Narrate everything during diapering, feeding, and dressing. Every word you say is vocabulary instruction. Every response you give to a cue is serve-and-return.
Step 4: Observe and write one thing down per day

Observation is not optional. It is how you know what children are learning and what they need next. You do not need to write paragraphs. Write one sentence per child per week at minimum. It can be on a sticky note, in a notebook, or in your phone notes app.

A good observation note has three parts:

  • What the child did (exactly)
  • What domain code it connects to
  • What you noticed about their level (are they just starting this skill? Getting better at it? Consistently doing it?)
Tip
Example observation note: 'Jaylen (AL.1.c) stayed at the block area for 25 minutes and rebuilt a tower four times after it fell. Consistently observed.' That is a complete assessment note.
Step 5: Connect with families every day

At pickup, tell every family one specific thing their child did. Not 'Jaylen had a good day.' But: 'Jaylen built the tallest block tower I have ever seen and rebuilt it four times when it fell. He is really persistent.' That is the difference between a daycare report and a curriculum partnership.

Section 4: A Full Scripted Sample Day - Preschool

This section walks you through a complete preschool day using re:Bloom. The theme for this sample day is Living Things. Every script is in the gold 'SAY' box. Action directions are in regular text. You can read these scripts word-for-word until you feel comfortable making them your own.

Remember
This is a guide, not a straitjacket. Children will go off-script. That is good. Follow their curiosity and come back to the plan when it makes sense.
7:30 AM - Arrival and Greeting

Stand at the door. Get down to child level. Make eye contact with every child who arrives. Use their name.

YOU SAY TO EACH CHILD:
Good morning, [Name]. I am so glad you are here. Do you know what we are going to explore today? Living things. Everything that is alive. Come look at what I put on the discovery table.
WHEN A CHILD PICKS UP A MAGNIFYING GLASS:
What do you see when you look through that? What does the worm look like up close? Is it moving? What do you think it is doing?
8:00 AM - Morning Meeting (15-20 minutes)

Gather children on the rug. Begin with your greeting song or ritual. Then move to the morning message.

Morning Message (write this on chart paper before children arrive):

Good morning, scientists! Today we are going to learn about living things. What makes something alive? Think about it!

READ THE MORNING MESSAGE ALOUD:
Let me read our morning message to you. I am going to point to each word as I read it. Watch my finger. 'Good morning, scientists!'

Take four to six responses. Do not evaluate them as right or wrong. Say 'That is interesting' or 'Tell me more' to every response. Write down what children say on a piece of chart paper labeled 'What We Think.'

INTRODUCE TODAY'S VOCABULARY WORDS:
I am going to teach you three special science words today. The first word is LIVING. Say that with me: living. Something that is living is alive. It grows, it breathes, it needs food or water. What is something living in this room right now?
THIRD VOCABULARY WORD:
The last word is INVESTIGATE. Say it: investigate. To investigate means to look carefully and find out. We are going to investigate living things today. Scientists investigate. You are all scientists today.
8:20 AM - Intentional Read-Aloud (15 minutes)

Choose a book about living things. Suggestions: 'What Do You Do With a Tail Like This?' by Jenkins and Page, 'Are You a Snail?' by Judith Allen, or any simple non-fiction book about animals or plants.

BEFORE OPENING THE BOOK:
Look at the cover of this book. Do not tell me what you see yet. Just look at it for ten seconds. Now: what do you notice? What do you think this book is going to be about? What makes you think that?
WHILE READING, STOP ON KEY PAGES AND ASK:
Look at this animal. What does its body look like? Do you think it lives in water or on land? Why do you think that? What do you think it eats?
AFTER THE BOOK:
Think about all the living things we saw in that book. Now think about living things in your neighborhood. What living things do you see near your home? Turn and tell the person next to you one living thing from your neighborhood.
8:40 AM - Phonological Awareness (10 minutes)

This is a dedicated 10-minute block for Science of Reading-aligned phonological awareness practice. Keep it playful, fast-paced, and fun.

Today's focus: Initial sounds (LL.2.b)
We are going to play a sound game. I am going to say a word and you tell me what sound it starts with. Ready? LIVING. What sound does living start with? /L/. Good. LEAF. /L/. LADYBUG. /L/. Do you notice something? All those words start with the same sound: /L/.
Now I am going to say a word that does NOT start with /L/ and see if you catch me. Ladybug... leaf... TREE. Did I sneak one in? What sound does tree start with? Right, /T/. Good listening!
MAKE IT PHYSICAL:
Let us stand up. Every time I say a word that starts with /S/ like SNAIL, you slither like a snake. Every time I say a word that starts with /B/ like BEE, you buzz like a bee. Ready? BEE... SNAIL... SNAKE... BIRD... SLUG. Be ready, I am going to go fast!
9:00 AM - Center Time and Play-Based Exploration (60-90 minutes)

This is the most important part of the day. Do not cut it short. Set up your centers with living things connections before children arrive. Here is what each center should have today:

CenterWhat to Put There Today
Science / DiscoveryLive plant, earthworm in soil, magnifying glasses, nature journals (blank paper stapled), crayons
Books / LibraryLiving things books, animal books, nature field guides appropriate for children
Art StudioPaper, paint in earth tones, leaf prints (press leaves with paint), branches to paint with
Blocks / BuildingAnimal figurines, small plants, rocks, building materials to make habitats
Dramatic PlayVeterinarian or garden center dramatic play materials
Writing CenterSimple printed outlines of animals children can label, pencils, crayons
Math ManipulativesSorting animals by type (pets, farm, wild), counting legs on animal pictures
AS CHILDREN CHOOSE CENTERS, CIRCULATE AND SAY THINGS LIKE:
What are you making? What kind of habitat is that? How many legs does that animal have? What do you think this creature eats?
WHEN A CHILD SEEMS STUCK OR BORED:
I wonder what would happen if you tried to make a home for that worm out of blocks. What would a worm's home look like? What does it need?
10:30 AM - Small Group Instruction (15-20 minutes per group)

Divide children into groups of four to six. While one group works with you, others continue center time. Today's small group focus is LL.1.b: vocabulary development.

INTRODUCE THE SMALL GROUP ACTIVITY:
We are going to play an investigation game. I have four pictures here. [Hold up pictures of a rock, a dog, a flower, and a pencil.] One of these things is different from the others. Look carefully. Which one is not alive? How do you know?
CLOSING THE SMALL GROUP:
I want each of you to pick up one object from the center and bring it back to our group. We are going to sort them together: living, once-living, or never-living.
11:15 AM - Outdoor Time (45 minutes minimum)
BEFORE GOING OUTSIDE:
We are going outside with a science mission today. You are going to be nature investigators. Your mission is to find five living things in our outdoor space. How will you know if something is alive? Right, it might be moving, growing, or it needs water or food. Let us go find them.
DURING OUTDOOR TIME, JOIN CHILDREN AND SAY:
What did you find? Tell me about it. How do you know it is alive? Where do you think it lives? What do you think it eats?
FIVE MINUTES BEFORE COMING IN:
Scientists, wrap up your investigation. When we get inside I am going to ask you to share the most interesting thing you found.
12:00 PM - Lunch
DURING LUNCH, CONNECT TO THE THEME:
Everything on your plate was once alive. Let us think about that. Your apple came from a living tree. Your bread came from wheat, which is a plant. Your milk came from a cow, which is alive. What else can you think of on your plate that was once alive?
12:45 PM - Rest or Quiet Time

Make the environment calm and predictable. Play quiet nature sounds or soft music. Children who do not sleep should have quiet independent activities: books, puzzles, or drawing. Stay calm and warm; your nervous system regulates theirs.

2:00 PM - Mathematics Focus (20 minutes)
GATHER CHILDREN FOR MATH:
We found a lot of living things today. Now we are going to sort and count them. I have pictures of all the living things we talked about today. Let us sort them by where they live: in the ground, in water, in the air, or on land.

After the group count, give each child a set of animal picture cards and ask them to create their own sorting system. Accept any reasonable sorting rule and ask children to explain their thinking.

2:30 PM - Closing Meeting
GATHER ON THE RUG:
Scientists, at the beginning of today I asked you: what makes something alive? Look at this chart where I wrote your ideas this morning. Let us read through them. Now that we have investigated all day, are there any ideas you want to change? Are there any new ideas you want to add?
INDIVIDUAL GOODBYE TO EACH CHILD:
[Name], you did something really interesting today. I noticed you [specific observation]. I will see you tomorrow.

Section 5: A Full Scripted Sample Day - Infant and Toddler

The infant and toddler day is organized around caregiving routines rather than structured lessons. Every diaper change, every feeding, and every floor play period is curriculum. This section walks through a full day showing exactly what to say and do during each caregiving moment.

Remember
There is no group schedule for infants. Each baby follows their own feeding and sleeping schedule. The scripts below are for individual caregiving moments, not group activities. The toddler sections assume a group of 10-14 toddlers with two caregivers.
INFANT SECTION: Birth to 12 Months
Arrival and Greeting
WHEN THE FAMILY ARRIVES:
Good morning, [Baby's name]. I am so glad you are here. [To the family] How was her night? Did she eat this morning? Is there anything I should know about today?
Tip
The arrival transition is the most important moment of the infant's day. Take your time. Do not rush the family. Do not rush the baby. Slow, warm, and predictable arrivals set the tone for the whole day.
Diapering (SE.4.a, LL.1.c, PW.2.b)

Every diaper change should be treated as a relationship-building and language-rich interaction. Put down your phone. Make eye contact. Talk the whole time.

AS YOU BEGIN:
[Baby's name], I am going to change your diaper now. I am going to lift you up. Ready? Up we go. Now I am laying you down on the changing table. Can you feel that? It is a little cool.
AS YOU FINISH:
All done. You did a great job. Let me wash my hands and then we can play. Do you want to look at the mirror? There you are!
Floor Play and Exploration (AL.1.a, CO.3.a, SE.4.a)

Set up your infant floor area before babies arrive. Use a variety of textures and materials: soft mats, a few simple cause-and-effect toys, a mirror at floor level, a simple mobile, board books within reach, and one or two natural materials like a smooth stone or a small plant in a secure pot.

AS YOU PLACE A BABY ON THE FLOOR:
[Name], here we go. Floor time! Look at all these interesting things. What are you going to explore first?
WHEN A BABY GETS FRUSTRATED:
I see you are working really hard on that. You want to get it. Let me move it just a little closer. There. Try again.
Tip
Serve and return is the most powerful thing you will do for an infant's brain development. Every time you respond to their communication - a sound, a look, a reach - you are building the neural architecture of language, cognition, and relationship. Aim for 3-4 complete serve-and-return exchanges per minute.
Feeding (PW.2.c, SE.4.a, LL.1.c)
AS YOU BEGIN A BOTTLE OR BREAST FEEDING PREPARATION:
[Name], are you hungry? I can see you are ready to eat. Let me get your bottle. Here it is. Can you feel that? I am going to hold you close now. There you go.
INTRODUCING SOLID FOODS (around 6 months):
[Name], today we are going to try something new. This is pureed sweet potato. It is orange. Can you see that? I am going to put a little bit on the spoon. Open wide. What do you think? That face! Is it good? Is it interesting? Let us try another bite.
TODDLER SECTION: 12 to 36 Months
Arrival and Morning Greeting (SE.4.a, LL.1.c)
AT THE DOOR AS EACH TODDLER ARRIVES:
Good morning, [Name]! You are here! I am so glad. Come in. Go find your cubby and hang up your bag. Then come show me one thing that is important to you today.
Tip
Do not rush a toddler through separation distress. Acknowledging the feeling first is always faster than trying to skip past it. A child who feels heard co-regulates more quickly than one who is redirected.
Morning Circle (LL, SE, AL) - 10 minutes maximum for toddlers

Toddler circle time should be very short: 8-10 minutes maximum. High movement, high participation, low sitting. Start with a song, add one brief activity, end with a transition song.

OPENING SONG (sing to the tune of any familiar melody):
Good morning to you, good morning to you, good morning dear [child's name], good morning to you! [Repeat for each child, using their name.] That is how we say hello at re:Bloom. We say EVERY person's name.
CLOSING TRANSITION:
It is center time! When I call your name, show me with your body where you want to go. [Child's name], where are you going? Show me with your feet. Go!
Floor Play and Center Time (60-90 minutes)

Toddler centers should be set up with large, accessible, safe materials. Everything on low shelves. Everything labeled with pictures and words. Everything with enough copies to reduce conflict.

NARRATING TODDLER PLAY:
[Name], I see you filling that bucket with sand. You filled it up. Now you are dumping it. Empty! Now you are filling it again. Full! [Pause] That bucket goes from empty to full and back to empty again. You are learning about capacity.
EXTENDING TODDLER LANGUAGE:
When a toddler says: 'More!' You say: 'More what? Can you tell me more? More playdough? More red? You want more red playdough. Here is more red playdough.' Always expand the utterance by one to two words more than the child said.
Lunch and Mealtimes (PW.2.c, SE, LL, MA)
AS TODDLERS SIT DOWN TO EAT:
Look at your plate. What colors do you see? Tell me one color. What do you think this is? Have you eaten this before? Let me tell you: this orange vegetable is called a sweet potato. Can you say that? Sweet potato.
FOR A TODDLER WHO REFUSES TO EAT:
[Name], you do not have to eat it. You can look at it. What color is it? What does it smell like? [Do not force. Do not use food as reward or punishment. Offer and allow refusal without reaction.] Okay, your body does not want that right now. That is okay.
Rest Time (PW.2.d, SE.2.a)
PREPARING FOR REST:
[Name], it is rest time. Let us get your cot and your blanket. I am going to play some quiet music. You can look at a book or close your eyes. I will be right here.
Afternoon and Departure (SE.4.a, LL)
AS FAMILIES ARRIVE FOR PICKUP:
[To the toddler first] Your [family member] is here! Let us go meet them. [To the family] Today [Name] did something really interesting. She spent almost 20 minutes at the water table pouring water between different sized cups. She was fascinated by figuring out which cup held more. I want to tell you that.

Section 6b: Using re:Bloom in a Family Child Care Home

FCC
This section addresses the Family Child Care setting specifically. If you work in a center-based program, you may skip this section or read it to understand how re:Bloom supports FCC providers.

Family child care is the only early childhood setting where an educator works with infants, toddlers, and preschoolers at the same time, in the same space, every day. This is not a limitation. It is one of the most developmentally rich environments that exists, and re:Bloom is designed to support it fully.

What Is Different About the FCC Setting

In a center-based classroom, you teach one age group. In a family child care home, you are simultaneously the primary caregiver for a baby, the secure base for a two-year-old, and the lead educator for a four-year-old. Everything in this section will help you use re:Bloom in that reality.

  • You have one Scope and Sequence document for all ages: the Family Child Care Track, which shows the birth-through-five developmental continuum in one view
  • The AI Lesson Plan Generator has a Family Child Care setting that builds plans where infants, toddlers, and preschoolers participate in every activity simultaneously
  • The Monthly Planning Guide is designed specifically for FCC, with mixed-age grouping strategies built into every month
  • Assessment uses the same Child Assessment Tool, but you will use a different form for each child based on their developmental level
The Mixed-Age Strategy: One Activity, Three Levels

The most important concept in re:Bloom FCC planning is the mixed-age strategy, noted as MIX throughout the curriculum. Every activity in re:Bloom FCC is designed so that infants, toddlers, and preschoolers can participate at the same time, each at their own level. You do not need three separate lesson plans. You need one activity that works on multiple levels at once.

EXAMPLE: Backyard Nature Walk
Infant: you carry them or place them on a blanket in the yard and narrate what you see. 'I see a leaf. It is yellow. Do you feel the breeze?' Toddler: they walk alongside and collect things in a small basket. 'You found a rock! Is it heavy or light?' Preschool: they carry a clipboard and draw or dictate what they observe. 'What do you notice? What are you wondering about?' One walk. Three levels of engagement. All nine domains addressed.
How to Structure Your FCC Day

Your daily structure with re:Bloom looks like this in a family child care home:

  • Arrival and greeting: greet every child by name at the door. Brief connection with families. Infant goes to their play space. Toddler and preschooler choose a morning activity.
  • Morning meeting (10 minutes): brief circle that works for all ages. A song, a weather observation, the day's plan. Infant on your lap or in a bouncy seat nearby.
  • Phonological awareness block (5-10 minutes): rhymes, syllable clapping, initial sound games. Infant hears the language. Toddler participates in the rhythm. Preschooler does the targeted skill work.
  • Choice time and outdoor time (60 or more minutes): child-directed exploration with materials connected to your current theme. This is the core of the re:Bloom FCC day.
  • Read-aloud (10-15 minutes): one book that works across age levels. Ask different questions to different children during the same book.
  • Caregiving routines: mealtimes, diapering, nap time, and rest. These are curriculum. Narrate what you are doing. Count the crackers. Name the feelings. Sing during transitions.
  • Closing meeting (5 minutes): brief recall. 'What did we explore today?' Preparation for family pickup.
Caregiving Routines Are Curriculum

In a center-based program, the curriculum is what happens between the routines. In a family child care home, the routines are the curriculum. Diapering is one-on-one language time. Mealtimes are social studies, mathematics, science, and language all at once. Nap time is a physical development standard. Do not think of these as interruptions to learning. They are the most important learning moments of the day.

Tip
When you are changing a diaper, narrate every step. Name body parts. Ask a question. Sing a specific song you always use. This consistency builds both language and secure attachment, the two most powerful outcomes of the first three years.
The Home Environment Is the Third Teacher

Your home environment is not a problem to apologize for. It is one of the richest learning environments available to young children. Real cooking develops mathematics and science. A garden develops biology and environmental stewardship. Household items become dramatic play props, math manipulatives, and science tools. The goal is not to make your home look like a classroom. It is to make your home intentional.

  • Book and literacy corner: books at child height, rotated to match your current theme
  • Art area: a table with accessible materials; process art only, no coloring sheets
  • Building and nature area: blocks, loose parts, and a windowsill or table with natural specimens
  • Cozy corner: a small calm space available to any child at any time; not a timeout, a regulation space
Tip
The cozy corner is one of the highest-impact changes you can make in a family child care home. A bean bag or a pile of cushions behind a low shelf, with a few soft materials and a calming item, gives every child in your group a regulation tool that costs almost nothing.
Working With Mixed Ages Without Losing Your Mind

The question family child care providers ask most often is: how do I plan for such a wide age range without burning out? The answer is that you stop trying to plan three separate curriculums and start planning one curriculum with three entry points. The FCC track in re:Bloom is built on this principle. Every activity has an infant layer, a toddler layer, and a preschool layer. You plan once. Everyone participates.

Remember
The infant in your care does not need a separate curriculum. They need a warm, language-rich environment with a consistent adult who narrates, responds, and connects. Everything else you are doing for the older children is already infant curriculum when you narrate it.
Documentation in a Family Child Care Setting

QRIS and licensing documentation is often the most stressful part of running a family child care home. re:Bloom is designed to make this easier, not harder. The FCC Monthly Planning Guide is your planning documentation. The completed lesson plans from the AI generator serve as curriculum implementation records. The Child Assessment Tool produces a printable QRIS-ready report for each child. A photograph with a brief observation note filed in a child's folder is anecdotal record documentation.

  • I use the FCC Monthly Planning Guide to document my curriculum plan each month

Section 7: Talking to Families

What families need from you

Families are not your audience. They are your partners. They know their child better than you do and you know child development and curriculum better than they do. Together you can give each child the best possible start. Here is what families need from you every day:

  • One specific, positive observation about their child
  • To know that you know and see their child as an individual
  • Information about what their child is working on developmentally
  • One simple thing they can do at home to support their child's learning
What to say at arrival and departure
Instead of saying this...Try saying this...
He had a good day.He spent 30 minutes at the block center building a bridge and was very focused. He is working on persistence and it really showed today.

Section 8: When Things Do Not Go as Planned

When a lesson falls apart

This will happen. A child will bring a frog to morning meeting and suddenly no one cares about your lesson plan. The right response is almost always: follow the frog. Child-led learning opportunities are curriculum. Here is how to stay connected to your learning objectives while following children's interests:

WHEN AN UNEXPECTED INTEREST TAKES OVER:
This is amazing. You found a frog. Let us look at it together. [Connect to your domain focus: if you were doing SC, investigate it. If you were doing LL, name its parts. If you were doing MA, count its legs.] How many legs does it have? Is it a living thing? How do we know? Where do you think it lives?
When a child's behavior is challenging

Challenging behavior is always communication. A child who is hitting is not a bad child; they are a child whose nervous system is overwhelmed and who does not yet have the skills to communicate distress another way. Here is the four-step response:

  • Safety first: if anyone is being hurt, get between them calmly and physically. Do not yell.
  • Name what you see without judgment: 'I see you hit [Name]. That tells me you are really frustrated right now.'
  • Name the feeling behind the behavior: 'You want the truck and you do not know how to get it. That is really hard.'
  • Teach the skill: 'Here is what you can say: Can I have a turn when you are done? Let us practice that together.'
Watch For
Never use time-out, removal of a privilege, or withdrawal of warmth as a response to emotional dysregulation. Research consistently shows these approaches increase dysregulation and damage the relationship that is the foundation of all learning.
When you are not sure what a standard means
WHEN YOU ARE CONFUSED:
Reread the standard statement. Then read the indicators. The indicators are the behaviors you will actually see. If you still do not understand, look at the Educator Strategies column. The strategies will make the standard concrete. If you are still stuck, reach out to re:Bloom support at rebloomcurriculum.com.

Section 9: Quick Reference Checklists

Before children arrive - check these every day
  • Room is safe: no hazards at child level, all exits clear
  • Centers are set up with intentional materials connected to current theme
  • There is something for all nine domains somewhere in the room
  • Morning message is written (preschool)
  • Books connected to the theme are visible and accessible
  • Sensory or science invitation is set up
  • Observation clipboard or notebook is ready
  • I know what I am focusing on today and why
During the day - do these every day
  • Greet every child by name at arrival
  • Read aloud at least once
  • Do a phonological awareness activity (preschool) or sing rhymes and songs (infants and toddlers)
  • Narrate during caregiving routines
  • Ask at least one open-ended question during play
  • Write one observation note about at least two children
  • Give every child at least one specific piece of positive verbal feedback
  • Have a genuine conversation with every child
At departure - do these every day
  • Tell every family one specific positive observation
  • Transfer any critical information about the child's day
  • Complete or review any observation notes from the day
  • Note what worked well and what you want to try differently tomorrow
  • Set out one thing for tomorrow's arrival discovery
